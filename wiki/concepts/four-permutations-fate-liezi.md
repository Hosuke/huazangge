## English

The "Four Permutations" (四可之命 or 四可) represents a sophisticated doctrinal formulation within the Liezi's chapter on Force and Fate, articulating the relationship between potentiality, actuality, and cosmic judgment through four paradigmatic scenarios concerning life and death.

The doctrine posits four possible relationships between capacity and fulfillment:

1. **"What can live, and indeed lives—this is heavenly blessing"**: Potential for life is actualized through natural processes aligned with the cosmos. This represents optimal fulfillment of one's natural capacity.

2. **"What can die, and indeed dies—this is heavenly blessing"**: Potential for death is actualized when circumstances warrant cessation. This too represents alignment with natural order, as continuing to exist would violate natural law.

3. **"What can live but does not live—this is heavenly punishment"**: Potential for life exists but circumstances prevent fulfillment. The individual is compelled to die despite having the capacity to live, representing a cosmic injustice or unnatural constraint.

4. **"What can die but does not die—this is heavenly punishment"**: Potential for death exists but circumstances compel continued existence. The individual is compelled to live despite having the capacity to die, representing an equally unnatural condition.

The philosophical innovation here is the recognition that both life and death may constitute either fortune or misfortune, depending entirely on whether circumstances allow natural capacities to be fulfilled. This framework rejects simplistic moral calculus—virtue does not guarantee life, nor does vice guarantee death.

The text elaborates: "Can live, can die, to live and to die, there are such cases; cannot live, cannot die, to die or to live, there are such cases." This emphasizes the contingency of outcomes: not every potential is actualized, and not every actualization represents the optimal path.

The concluding cosmological section synthesizes these permutations into a grand vision: "Birth and death, neither things nor self, are all fate—what wisdom cannot contend with. Therefore it is said: The obscurely infinite, the Way gathers itself; the indifferently undifferentiated, the Way moves itself. Heaven and Earth cannot transgress this, sagely wisdom cannot interfere, ghosts and spirits cannot deceive. The natural one silently completes, levels and stills, sends forth and welcomes."
