Definition: Arhatship (Pali: arahant; Sanskrit: arhat; Chinese: 阿罗汉; Japanese: 阿羅漢) denotes the state of a perfected saint who has achieved liberation from samsara by eradicating all defilements.

Etymology: "Arhat" means "worthy one" or "one who has conquered the enemies of the mind." In early Buddhist texts, the term is used interchangeably with "savaka" (disciple) and "pratyekabuddha".

Stages: According to the four‑stage model, the last stage is arhat. The preceding stages are sotapanna (stream‑enterer), sakadagamin (once‑returner), and anagami (non‑returner). An arhat has destroyed all ten fetters and is completely free from future rebirth.

Attributes: An arhat possesses the three kinds of true knowledge (tri‑jnana) and the four bases of psychic power (iddhipada). He/she can manifest various supramundane abilities but is no longer bound to use them for personal benefit. The Buddhist canon lists ten qualities of an arahant: perfect moral discipline, equanimity, mindfulness, concentration, wisdom, etc.

Arhatship in Theravada: In the Pali Canon, arhatship is the ultimate goal for the "savaka" path. The arahant is revered as a teacher and a model for lay and monastic practice. The emphasis is on personal liberation through insight (vipassana) and the eradication of lust, hatred, and delusion.

Arhatship in Mahayana: Mahayana teachings often regard arhatship as a provisional or inferior goal compared to the bodhisattva ideal. Some sutras, like the Lotus Sutra, reinterpret the arhat as a "lesser vehicle" (hinayana) that still leads to liberation but lacks the great compassion required for full buddhahood. In contrast, the Yogacara and Madhyamaka schools preserve the concept as a valid stage on the path to full enlightenment, sometimes called "pratyekabuddha" or "solitary Buddha."

Historical development: Over centuries, the concept evolved from a simple designation for a liberated disciple to a broader category that includes a wide range of abilities and roles in the Buddhist community. In East Asian Buddhism, arhats (罗汉) are often depicted in art as protectors of the Dharma and are venerated in temples.

Contemporary relevance: While many modern Buddhist practitioners aim for awakening, the ideal of arhatship remains an important reference point for understanding the spectrum of Buddhist soteriological goals.
