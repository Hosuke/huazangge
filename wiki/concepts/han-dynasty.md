## English

### Overview
The Han dynasty, lasting from 202 BC to 220 AD, succeeded the short‑lived Qin dynasty and preceded the Three Kingdoms period. It is divided into the Western (or Former) Han (202 BC – 9 AD) and the Eastern (or Later) Han (23 AD – 220 AD). The dynasty’s reign is celebrated as a formative era that shaped Chinese identity, language, and statecraft.

### History
- **Founding and Western Han**: Liu Bang, a peasant rebel, proclaimed himself Emperor Gaozu in 202 BC after the collapse of the Qin. The early Han rulers consolidated power, adopted Confucianism as state ideology, and reformed the legal code.
- **Emperor Wu’s expansion**: Under Emperor Wu (141–87 BC), the empire expanded dramatically into the Western Regions, Central Asia, and the Korean peninsula, establishing the famous Silk Road trade routes.
- **Wang Mang’s interregnum (9–23 AD)**: A brief usurpation attempted radical reforms, leading to social upheaval and eventual collapse.
- **Eastern Han restoration**: The dynasty was restored by Liu Xiu (Emperor Guangwu), who moved the capital to Luoyang, fostering a period of recovery and patronage of arts and science.

### Government and Administration
- **Central bureaucracy**: A three‑tiered system of central ministries, regional commanderies (郡), and local districts (县) administered the empire.
- **Meritocratic examinations**: Confucian scholars were recruited through imperial examinations, establishing a merit‑based civil service that endured for centuries.
- **Legal and fiscal reforms**: The Han codified laws, standardized weights and measures, and introduced a land‑tax system that supported both the state and peasantry.

### Economy and Trade
- **Agricultural surplus**: Advances in iron smelting, irrigation, and the spread of the two‑season rice cultivation increased yields, supporting population growth.
- **Silk Road**: The opening of the Silk Road facilitated trade with the Roman Empire, Central Asia, and the Indian subcontinent, bringing luxury goods, new technologies, and Buddhism into China.
- **Currency and markets**: The Han standardized copper coinage (banliang) and established state‑run markets (shi) that regulated commerce.

### Culture and Science
- **Philosophy and religion**: Confucianism became the dominant ideology, while Daoism and early Buddhist practices gained popularity.
- **Literature and historiography**: Sima Qian compiled the *Records of the Grand Historian* (Shiji), setting the model for Chinese historiography. Poetry, especially the *nineteen old poems*, flourished.
- **Technological innovations**: The invention of paper (c. 105 AD attributed to Cai Lun), the seismometer (Zhang Heng), and advances in metallurgy, medicine, and astronomy marked the era.

### Legacy
- **Identity**: The Han ethnicity, language (Mandarin’s “Han” characters), and cultural traditions trace their roots to this period.
- **Political model**: The Han administrative system and meritocratic examination concept influenced Chinese governance for two millennia.
- **Cultural influence**: Confucian classics, legal codes, and artistic motifs from the Han period became benchmarks for later dynasties.

## 中文

### 概览
汉朝（前202年—220年）是中国历史上第二个帝国王朝，分为西汉（前202年—9年）和东汉（23年—220年）。它结束了秦的短暂统治，开启了中华文明的黄金时期，奠定了后世政治、文化和经济的框架。

### 历史沿革
- **建国与西汉**：刘邦（前202年称帝）为汉高祖，建立西汉，定都长安（后迁至洛阳）。初期皇帝推行休养生息、轻徭薄赋的政策，逐步巩固统治。
- **汉武帝的扩张**：汉武帝时期（前141年—前87年）疆域大幅扩展，向西域、中亚、朝鲜半岛及岭南派遣使者，开通丝绸之路，设立都护府管理西域。
- **王莽篡汉（9—23年）**：王莽推行改革，引发社会动荡，最终被刘秀（东汉光武帝）推翻，汉朝复辟。
- **东汉复兴**：光武帝迁都洛阳，恢复经济和文化秩序，出现“光武中兴”。

### 政治制度
- **中央官制**：设立三公九卿制，后演变为尚书省、中书省、门下省等机构，形成三省六部的雏形。
- **科举雏形**：通过举孝廉、博士弟子等选拔官员，奠定后世科举制度的基础。
- **法律与财政**：修订《九章律》，统一度量衡，实施土地税和人口税，确保国家财政收入。

### 经济与贸易
- **农业进步**：推广铁制农具、灌溉技术、双季稻种植，提升粮食产量，支持人口增长。
- **丝绸之路**：打通东西方商路，输出丝绸、瓷器等奢侈品，输入香料、宝石、佛教等外来文化。
- **货币与市场**：统一铸造五铢钱，设立官市，管理商品流通。

### 文化与科技
- **哲学宗教**：儒家思想成为官方意识形态；道家、佛教逐渐传播，对后世宗教格局产生深远影响。
- **史学与文学**：司马迁《史记》开创纪传体史书；汉赋、乐府诗等文学形式繁荣。
