## English

The concept of **Junzi** (君子), often translated as "noble person" or "gentleman," is central to Confucian philosophy. Confucius describes the Junzi as someone who embodies moral integrity, wisdom, and is committed to virtuous actions. The Junzi is expected to focus on self-cultivation and personal morality, exemplifying the ideal qualities such as benevolence and righteousness. 

The Junzi is contrasted with the "small man" (小人), who is driven by self-interest and lacks moral depth. The teachings suggest that the Junzi seeks to cultivate their character rather than seek external recognition or rewards. This is evident in the passage where Confucius states, "君子求诸己，小人求诸人", highlighting that a Junzi looks within for self-improvement while a small person looks to others.

In governance, the Junzi should embody exemplary behavior, thus serving as a model for society. This is reiterated through teachings regarding governance and moral duty, where the Junzi is urged to act with integrity, ensuring justice and benevolence in their leadership. The Junzi's ability to offer ministerial advice reflects their wisdom and dedication to ethical governance, as discussed in earlier teachings.

## English

# Junzi (君子)

The **junzi** (君子), often translated as "gentleman," "superior person," or "exemplary person," represents the Confucian ideal of moral cultivation and ethical excellence. In the Analects, Confucius establishes the junzi as the archetype of perfected human character.

## Core Characteristics

The junzi embodies several key virtues:

**Moral Constancy**: "人不知而不慍，不亦君子乎?" (When others don't recognize your merits, are you not vexed? Is this not the mark of a junzi?) This demonstrates the junzi's inner stability and lack of concern for external validation.

**Fundamental Virtues**: "君子務本，本立而道生" (The junzi devotes himself to the fundamentals. When the root is established, the Way will grow therefrom). The junzi focuses on cultivating foundational virtues like [[benevolence]] and filial piety.

**Gravitas and Learning**: "君子不重則不威，學則不固" (If the junzi is not grave, he will not inspire awe, and his learning will not be on a firm foundation). This emphasizes the connection between dignity and genuine [[knowledge-learning]].

## Relationship to Other Concepts

The junzi's cultivation of virtue connects to [[confucian-self-examination]] through daily moral reflection. Their approach to governance reflects principles of [[benevolent-government]], while their educational philosophy informs [[knowledge-learning]].

## Development Through Practice

Confucius emphasizes that becoming a junzi requires continuous effort: "君子食無求飽，居無求安，敏於事而慎於言，就有道而正焉" (The junzi does not seek satiety in eating, nor comfort in dwelling. He is diligent in affairs and careful in speech, and he seeks out those who possess the Way in order to be set straight by them).

孔子强调成为君子需要持续努力：「君子食無求飽，居無求安，敏於事而慎於言，就有道而正焉」表明君子超越物质追求，勤勉做事，谨慎言语，并主动向有道之人学习。

## English

### The Confucian Gentleman

The junzi (君子) represents the Confucian ideal of the morally cultivated individual who embodies virtue and acts according to moral principles. As Confucius describes: "The Master said, 'The superior man in everything considers righteousness to be essential'" (Analects 15.18).

### Characteristics of the Junzi

Key attributes include:
- Moral consistency: "The superior man does not, even for the space of a single meal, act contrary to benevolence" (Analects 4.5)
- Righteous orientation: "The Master said, 'The mind of the superior man is conversant with righteousness; the mind of the mean man is conversant with gain'" (Analects 4.16)
- Universal approach: "The Master said, 'The superior man, in the world, does not set his mind either for anything, or against anything; what is right he will follow'" (Analects 4.10)

### Junzi vs. Petty Person

The contrast between junzi and xiaoren (小人) highlights moral distinctions:
- "The Master said, 'The superior man thinks of virtue; the small man thinks of comfort. The superior man thinks of the sanctions of law; the small man thinks of favors which he may receive'" (Analects 4.11)
- This distinction reflects the [[confucian-ethics]] emphasis on moral character over material concerns.

## 中文

### 君子之理想

君子乃儒家道德修養之典範，體現美德並依道德原則行事。孔子曰：「君子喻於義，小人喻於利」（《論語·里仁》），明確區分君子與小人之道德取向。

### 君子之特質

君子具備以下特質：
- 道德一致性：「君子無終食之間違仁，造次必於是，顛沛必於是」（《論語·里仁》）
- 義之取向：「君子之於天下也，無適也，無莫也，義之與比」（《論語·里仁》）
- 德行優先：「君子懷德，小人懷土；君子懷刑，小人懷惠」（《論語·里仁》）

### 君子與小人之別

君子與小人之對比凸顯道德差異，反映[[confucian-ethics]]對道德品格之重視勝於物質利益。

The junzi (君子) is Confucianism's ideal personality:
- Embodies [[Ren]] and [[Li]] perfectly
- "The gentleman is not a vessel" (Analects 2.12) - has broad moral capacity
- Contrasts with xiaoren (小人 petty person)
