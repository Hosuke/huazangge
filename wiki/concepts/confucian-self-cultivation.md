## English

### Introduction
**Confucian Self‑Cultivation** (儒家自我修养) refers to the disciplined process by which individuals develop moral virtue, refine their character, and align themselves with the greater order of humanity and the cosmos. Rooted in the teachings of Confucius (孔子, Kongzi) and elaborated by later thinkers such as Mencius (孟子) and Xunzi (荀子), self‑cultivation is not a private hobby but a public responsibility aimed at realizing the ideal of the *junzi* (君子), the exemplary person.

### Historical Development
- **Confucius (551–479 BC)** emphasized the importance of *xiūshēn* (修身, self‑cultivation) through learning, reflection, and the practice of *lǐ* (礼, ritual propriety). He taught that moral growth begins with personal discipline and spreads outward to family, state, and the world.
- **Mencius (372–289 BC)** expanded the notion by highlighting the innate “four beginnings” (四端) – *ren* (仁, benevolence), *yi* (义, righteousness), *li* (礼, propriety), and *zhi* (智, wisdom) – as seeds that must be nurtured through conscious effort.
- **Xunzi (c. 310–c. 237 BC)** stressed the role of *xué* (学, learning) and *sī* (思, thinking) in transforming raw human nature, arguing that moral order is achieved through external guidance and inner self‑discipline.
- **Neo‑Confucianism (Song‑Ming dynasties)** further systematized self‑cultivation, integrating it with the concepts of *cheng* (诚, sincerity) and *zhonghe* (中和, central harmony) as described in the *Doctrine of the Mean (Zhongyong)* and the *Great Learning (Daxue)*.

### Core Components
1. **Learning and Study** – Mastery of the *Four Books* (四书) and the *Five Classics* (五经) provides the intellectual foundation.
2. **Ritual Practice (lǐ)** – Ceremonial acts, social etiquette, and daily rites serve as external forms that shape inner virtue.
3. **Self‑Reflection and Examination** – Regular introspection (反省) aligns actions with moral principles.
4. **Cultivation of Moral Emotions** – Developing *ren* (humaneness), *yi* (righteousness), and *xin* (trustworthiness) strengthens the affective basis of ethical behavior.
5. **Social Relationships** – The five cardinal relationships (君臣、父子、夫妇、兄弟、朋友) are arenas where self‑cultivation is exercised and tested.

### The Process: From Self to World
Confucian pedagogy describes a stepwise expansion:
1. **Self (修己)** – Rectifying one’s own heart and conduct.
2. **Family (齐家)** – Harmonizing familial duties, especially filial piety (*xiao*).
3. **State (治国)** – Serving the ruler with moral governance.
4. **World (平天下)** – Contributing to universal peace and order.

### Key Texts
- *Great Learning (Daxue)* – Outlines the sequential path of self‑cultivation.
- *Doctrine of the Mean (Zhongyong)* – Emphasizes *cheng* (sincerity) as the core of inner harmony.
- *Analects (Lunyu)* – Provides the foundational sayings on virtue and practice.
- *Mencius* – Expounds the potential for moral growth within all humans.
