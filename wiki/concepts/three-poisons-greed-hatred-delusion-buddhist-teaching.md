## English

The *Shengjing* (生經) systematically illustrates the Buddhist doctrine of the three poisons—greed (貪), hatred (瞋), and delusion (癡)—through a series of interconnected narratives, each demonstrating how these mental defilements bind beings to the cycle of rebirth and obstruct enlightenment. Far from abstract psychological categories, the three poisons in these sutras emerge as concrete forces that manifest in specific actions, relationships, and karmic consequences across multiple lifetimes.

**Greed (貪)** finds vivid expression in the *My-Own Bird* narrative, where attachment to possessions—symbolized by the bird's futile claim over mountain fruit—leads to suffering and death. The wealthy man of the same sutra exemplifies worldly greed: he accumulated inexhaustible treasure yet lived in poverty, refusing to feed his family or practice generosity. Upon death without heirs, his wealth reverted to the state. The sutra explicitly parallels this man's fate with the bird's, demonstrating that greed produces identical results regardless of whether one is human or animal.

**Hatred (瞋)** appears most prominently in the narratives concerning Devadatta and his cohorts. The story of the turtle and monkey reveals how the turtle's wife, filled with jealousy and malice toward the monkey, engineered the betrayal that ended their friendship and established karmic patterns persisting into future incarnations. Similarly, the narrative of the five ascetics shows how hasty curses spoken in anger create karmic seeds that ripen across lifetimes.

**Delusion (癡)** underlies all these cases: the bird's delusion lies in believing that it possessed what was never truly its own; the wealthy man's delusion consists of failing to recognize impermanence and the uselessness of hoarding; the turtle's wife's delusion manifests in jealousy that distorts reality and leads to harmful action.

The *Shengjing* teaches that the three poisons are mutually reinforcing and generate an endless cycle of suffering. They are not merely negative mental states to be suppressed but fundamental errors in perception that color all experience. Liberation requires not just behavioral reform but a fundamental transformation of understanding—the development of wisdom (慧) that perceives the true nature of phenomena as impermanent, empty, and interdependent.

## 中文

《生經》透過一系列相互關聯的敘事，系統地闡明了佛教的三毒教義——貪、瞋、癡——每個故事都展示了這些心靈汙染如何將眾生束縛於輪迴之中並阻礙覺悟。三毒在這些經文中遠非抽象的心理分類，而是具體的力量，表現在特定的行為、人際關係及跨越多世的業報之中。

**貪**在「我所鳥」物語中得到了生動的表達：對財產的執著——以鳥兒對山中果實的徒勞聲稱為象徵——導致痛苦與死亡。同經文中那位富商的案例代表了世間的貪欲：他積累了無盡的財寶，卻過著貧窮的生活，既不肯養活家人，也不實踐布施。命終無嗣，財產沒入官府。經文明確將此人的命運與我所鳥的命運相比，說明無論是人還是動物，貪的結果並無不同。

**瞋**最突出地表現在關於調達及其同夥的物語中。鱉獼猴的故事揭示了鱉妻對獼猴充滿嫉妒與惡意，如何策劃了結束他們友誼的背叛，並建立了持續到未來世的業力模式。同樣地，五仙人物語展示了如何在憤怒中匆忙說出的咒詛，創造了跨越生生世世成熟的業力種子。

**癡**作為根本無明，是所有這些案例的基礎：鳥的癡在於相信它擁有的東西從來不是真正屬於自己的；富商的癡在於未能認識無常及囤積的無用性；鱉妻的癡在於嫉妒扭曲了現實並導致有害的行為。

《生經》教導：三毒相互增強，生成無盡的苦惱循環。它們不僅是需要抑制的負面心理狀態，而是滲透一切經驗的根本認知錯誤。解脫不僅需要行為的改革，更需要理解的根本轉變——發展能洞察諸法實相為無常、空、無自性的智慧。
