## English

The principle of **"Non-Injury as Foundation"** (不傷為本) represents one of the most fundamental teachings in Daoist cultivation as articulated by Ge Hong in the Baopuzi. This principle asserts that the preservation of one's original qi and the avoidance of self-inflicted damage constitute the absolute prerequisite for any successful cultivation practice.

### Core Principles of Non-Injury

The human body, according to this teaching, exists in a state of inherent fragility. Ge Hong employs the striking analogy of the **mulan yangliu** (木槿楊柳) — a species of shubbery that, though possessing remarkable regenerative capacity, will nonetheless wither if its roots are shallow, subjected to constant carving and peeling, or if its essential sap cannot properly circulate. Similarly, the human form is "easily injured, difficult to nurture" (易傷難養), far more so than any plant.

The teaching identifies numerous sources of self-inflicted harm:

- **Excessive exertion**: Capabilities that exceed one's limits, forced movement, extreme fatigue
- **Emotional extremes**: Excessive joy or sorrow, lingering depression, anxious worry
- **Excessive desire**: Endless pursuit of desires, constant craving
- **Improper rest**: Inconsistent sleep patterns, excessive talking or laughter
- **Physical strain**: Overwork, archery, heavy lifting
- **Digestive disturbances**: Overeating, immediate reclining after meals, excessive consumption of cold foods
- **Sexual excess**: Without restraint in yin-yang circulation
- **Neglect of balance**: Failure to properly modulate exposure to cold and heat

### The Doctrine of Gradual Harm

A crucial insight of this principle is the recognition that harmful practices often proceed imperceptibly. As Ge Hong explains: "The harmful are like the consumption of oil by a lamp — one does not perceive it, yet suddenly it is exhausted." Conversely, beneficial practices develop gradually: "The beneficial are like the growth of seedlings — one does not sense it, yet suddenly they flourish."

This asymmetry between the rapidity of harm and the slowness of benefit underscores the necessity of proactive protection. The principle warns against the attitude that minor injuries can be ignored: "Do not, because a small benefit seems insignificant, fail to cultivate it; do not, because a small injury seems harmless, fail to guard against it."

### The Foundation Before the Summit

The teaching establishes a clear progression: **first establish the foundation of non-injury, then seek advancement**. Only after ensuring that one's qi and blood are not being depleted can one meaningfully pursue the higher practices of immortality. Those who attempt to engage in cultivation while their fundamental constitution is already severely damaged are likened to attempting to add water to a leaking vessel — no matter how much is poured in, it cannot be filled.
