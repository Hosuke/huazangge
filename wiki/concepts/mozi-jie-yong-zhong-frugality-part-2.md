## English

### The Philosophy of Frugality in Mozi - "Frugality Part 2" (節用中)

The chapter "Frugality Part 2" (節用中) from the Mozi presents a systematic exposition of the practical governance principles practiced by the ancient sage kings (聖王). Unlike purely moral or ritualistic approaches to governance, this text emphasizes pragmatic utility and economic moderation as foundations for successful rule.

#### The Foundation: Loving the People and Demonstrating Benefit

Mozi opens by articulating the fundamental reason why ancient sage kings were able to "rule the world and rectify the feudal lords" (王天下、正諸侯). The text identifies two interrelated principles:

1. **Loving the people diligently and being sincere in loyalty** (愛民謹忠，利民謹厚)
2. **Connecting loyalty and trust together, and showing the people profit** (忠信相連，又示之以利)

The result of such governance was that the people were "never satisfied throughout their lives, and never weary even unto death" (終身不饜，歿世而不卷). This passage establishes that for Mozi, effective governance requires both moral virtue (loyalty and love) and material benefit—the two must work together.

#### The Core Principle of Frugality

The central doctrine articulated throughout the chapter is: **"加費不加民利者，聖王弗為"** — "If something adds expense without benefiting the people, the sage kings would not do it."

This principle serves as the evaluative standard for all governance decisions. Every expenditure must be justified by its contribution to the welfare of the common people.

#### The System of Organized Labor

The text describes how the sage kings organized society so that "all craftsmen under heaven" (天下群百工)—including wheelwrights, carriage makers, leather workers, potters, smelters, and carpenters—"each followed what they were capable of doing" (各從其事其所能). This division of labor ensured efficient production while preventing wasteful competition.

The criterion for such organized activity was: "Whatever is sufficient to provide for the people's needs—if adding cost without adding benefit to the people, it should cease" (凡足以奉給民用，諸加費不加民利則止).

#### Regulations for Food and Drink

Regarding diet, the sage kings established that food and drink should be:
- **Sufficient to fill the empty and continue the breath** (充虛繼氣)
- **Able to strengthen the limbs** (强股肱)
- **Capable of making ears and eyes sharp** (使耳目聰明)

The text explicitly rejects elaborate cuisine: "They did not pursue the extreme refinement of the five flavors, the harmony of fragrant aromatics, nor rare and strange things from distant lands" (不極五味之調、芬香之和，不致遠國珍怪異物).

The example of Yao is invoked: despite ruling south to Jiaozhi and north to Youdu, east to where the sun rises and west to where it sets—having all under submission—his personal simplicity was such that he ate from earthen bowls, drank from earthen vessels, and used simple ladles for measuring.

#### Regulations for Clothing

The clothing regulations follow the same principle of functional moderation:
- **Winter garments of dark blue or black fabric should be light yet warm** (冬服紺緅之衣輕且暖)
- **Summer garments of coarse cloth should be light yet cool** (夏服絺綌之衣輕且凊)

Elaboration that does not increase the people's benefit was rejected.

#### Military Equipment

For defense against "fierce birds and beasts, and those who harm the people" (猛禽狡獸暴人害民), the sage kings developed:

**Swords**: "When worn at the side, they can pierce when thrust and cut when struck, yet the blade does not break even when struck from the side—this is the advantage of the sword."

**Armor**: "When worn as clothing, it is light yet strong; movement is unimpeded yet protected—this is the advantage of armor."

**Chariots**: "For heavy loads and distant travel; safe to ride and easy to pull; safe in that it does not injure people; advantageous in that it brings swift arrival—this is the advantage of chariots."

**Boats**: "For rivers and valleys too large to cross by land; sufficient to transport; even the highest rulers (三公諸侯) used the same boats; the ferrymen made no special preparations—this is the advantage of boats."

#### Moderate Burial Practices (節葬)

The text advocates for simplified burial to avoid burdening the living:
- **Shrouds of three sets, sufficient to decay with the flesh** (衣三領，足以朽肉)
- **Coffins three inches thick, sufficient to decay with the bones** (棺三寸，足以朽骸)
- **Tombs deep enough not to reach springs and not to release noxious gases** (堀穴深不通於泉，流不發洩)
- **After burial, the living should not observe prolonged mourning** (死者既葬，生者毋久喪用哀)

The text grounds this practice in historical precedent: when people had no palaces, they lived in caves (堀穴) on hills. The sage kings improved upon this by building palaces to protect from wind, cold, rain, and summer dampness and heat.

#### Principles of Architecture (宮室之法)

For housing, the sage kings established that a proper dwelling should:
- **Have sides that can ward off wind and cold** (旁可以圉風寒)
- **Have a roof that can ward off snow, frost, rain, and dew** (上可以圉雪霜雨露)
- **Have an interior that is clean enough for sacrificial offerings** (中可以祭祀)
- **Have walls sufficient to separate men and women** (宮墻足以為男女之別)

Again, the principle applies: "Adding cost without adding benefit to the people, the sage kings would not do."

#### Historical Context and Significance

This chapter represents Mozi's practical approach to governance, distinct from the ritual emphasis of Confucianism. While Mozi does not reject ritual entirely, he subordinates it to functional utility. The sage kings of antiquity succeeded not through elaborate ceremonies but through ensuring the material welfare of their subjects through efficient, moderate, and purposeful governance.

The text also reveals Mozi's historical consciousness—he repeatedly invokes Yao, Shun, and Yu as exemplars of proper governance, arguing that his proposed reforms are actually returns to the authentic practices of the ancient sage kings rather than innovations.

## 中文

### 墨子「節用中」篇：聖王節用之道

「節用中」篇是《墨子》的重要篇章，系統闡述了古代聖王的節用治國之道。與純粹道德說教或繁文縟節不同，墨子在此強調實用功利與節儉適度作為治國之根本。

#### 愛民示利：聖王成功之本

墨子開篇即揭示古代聖王能夠「王天下、正諸侯」的根本原因：

1. **愛民謹忠，利民謹厚**——誠摯地愛護人民，厚重地為民謀利
2. **忠信相連，又示之以利**——忠信相連，並以實際利益展示於民

如此治國之結果，乃是「終身不饜，歿世而不卷」——人民終身不滿足，終世而不厭倦。此段確立了墨子治國觀的核心：有效治理需要道德美德（忠與愛）與物質利益並重，二者不可偏廢。

#### 節用之核心原則

貫穿全篇的核心準則為：**「加費不加民利者，聖王弗為」**——凡增加費用而不增加民利之事，聖王不為。此原則成為衡量一切政務的標準：每一項開支都必須以是否有利於百姓為準則。

#### 百工分業之道

聖王以系統方式組織社會，使「天下群百工——輪、車、鞼、匏、陶、冶、梓、匠——各從事其所能」。此種分工確保效率生產，避免浪費競爭。其標準為：「凡足以奉給民用，諸加費不加民利則止。」

#### 飲食之法

聖王規定飲食之道：
- **充虛繼氣**——足以填補空虛、延續氣息
- **强股肱**——强健四肢
- **使耳目聰明**——使耳目靈敏

墨子明確反對精緻美食：「不極五味之調、芬香之和，不致遠國珍怪異物。」其以堯為例：雖南撫交阯、北降幽都、東西至日所出入，莫不賓服，然其個人生活極度簡樸——飯於土塯，啜於土形，斗以酌。

#### 衣服之法

冬服「紺緅之衣輕且暖」，夏服「絺綌之衣輕且凊」。「加費不加於民利者，聖王弗為。」

#### 兵器與交通

墨子詳述防禦「猛禽狡獸暴人害民」之所需：

**劍**：日帶劍，「為刺則入，擊則斷，旁擊而不折」——此劍之利。

**甲**：甲為衣，「輕且利，動則兵且從」——此甲之利。

**車**：為服重致遠，「乘之則安，引之則利，安以不傷人，利以速至」——此車之利。

**舟楫**：越大川廣谷，「足以將之則止」，「雖上者三公諸侯至，舟楫不易，津人不飾」——此舟之利。

#### 節葬之法

墨子主張簡葬以免生者負累：
- **衣三領，足以朽肉**
- **棺三寸，足以朽骸**
- **堀穴深不通於泉，流不發洩，則止**
- **死者既葬，生者毋久喪用哀**

#### 宮室之法

聖王規定宮室之標準：
- **旁可以圉風寒**——兩側可擋風寒
- **上可以圉雪霜雨露**——屋頂可遮雪霜雨露
- **中可以祭祀**——室內清潔可祭祀
- **宮墻足以為男女之別**——牆壁足以分隔男女

#### 歷史意義

此篇代表墨子實用主義治國之道，區別於儒家之禮儀強調。墨子不排斥禮儀，但將其置於功能效用之下。聖王之成功，非賴繁複禮儀，而在於通過節儉適度、務實有為之治國方式確保百姓物質福利。

墨子屢以堯舜禹為楷模，表明其主張非創新而是復歸古聖王之正道。

## 日本語

### 墨子「節用中」篇——聖王の節用之道

「節用中」は『墨子』における重要な篇章であり、古代聖王の節用による統治の原則を体系的に阐述したものである。純粋な道徳的説教や複雑な儀礼とは異なり，墨子はここで実用的功利と倹約的中道を統治の根本として強調する。

#### 愛民示利——聖王成功の根本

墨子は冒頭で、古代聖王が「天下を王し、諸侯を正す」ことができた根本原因を明らかにする：

1. **愛民謹忠，利民謹厚**——民を愛し、厚く民のために利益を図る
2. **忠信相連，又示之以利**——忠信相连し、実利を持って民に示す

この統治の結果は「終身饜かず、没世して倦まざる」ことであった。この箇所は、有効な統治には道徳的徳目（忠と愛）と物質的利益の両立が必要であることを確立する。

#### 節用の核心原則

全篇を通じて貫かれる中核原理は：**「加費不加民利者、聖王弗為」**——費用を増すだけで民に利益を加えないことは、聖王は行わない、というものである。この原則は全ての国政を評価する基準となる：全ての出費は民に有利であるかどうかで判断される。

#### 百工分業の道

聖王は社会を組織し、「天下の群百工——輪、车、鞼、匏、陶、冶、梓、匠——各自その能力に応じた」活動に従事させた。この分業は効率的な生産を保証し、無駄な競争を防ぐ。その基準は「凡そ民用に奉給する足以けば、諸に費を加して民利を加えざれば則ち止む」であった。

#### 飲食之法

聖王の飲食規定：
- **充虛繼氣**——空虛を満たし、気を継ぐに足る
- **強股肱**——四肢を強くする
- **耳目聡明ならしむ**——耳目を鋭くする

墨子は精緻な料理を明確に拒否する：「五味の調整の極み、香気の调和の極み、遠国からの珍奇異物の到来を求めず」と。堯の例を挙げる：南は交阯に撫で、北は幽都に降り、東西は日の出入りする所に至り、、全てが服属した에도拘わらず、个人生活は極めて質素であった——土塯で飯し、土形ですくい、斗で計った。

#### 衣服之法

冬は「紺緅の衣軽くして且つ暖かく」、夏は「絺綌の衣軽くして且つ涼しい」。「費を加して民利を加えざる者は、聖王は行わざる」と。

#### 兵器と交通

墨子は「猛禽狡獣暴人害民」の防御に必要なものを詳述する：

**剣**：劍を帯び、「刺すにおいては入り、撃つにおいては断ち、旁撃しても折不れ」——これが剣の利である。

**鎧**：鎧を衣とすれば「軽くして且つ利く、動けば兵且つ從う」——これが鎧の利である。

**車**：重荷を運んで遠くに至り、「乗れば則ち安く、引けば則ち利く、安きは人以って傷つけず、利きは速やかに至る」——これが車の利である。

**舟楫**：大河谷を渡るには「将くに足をれば則ち止む」、「上位者三公諸侯が来るも、舟楫は改めず、津人は飾りせず」——これが舟の利である。

#### 節葬之法

墨子は簡葬を主張し、生者の負担を避ける：
- **衣三領足以朽肉す**
- **棺三寸足以朽骸す**
- **堀穴は泉に通ぜず、流を発洩せざるを深くし、則ち止む**
- **死者は既に葬り、生者は久しく喪を用ひて哀しまず**

#### 宮室之法

聖王の宮室の基準：
- **旁は以って風寒を圉うに足る**
- **前は以って雪霜雨露を圉うに足る**
- **中は祭祀に清潔なるに足る**
- **宮墻は以って男女の別を為すに足る**

#### 歴史的意義

本篇は墨子の実用的統治哲学を代表し、儒家の高級な儀礼重視と区別される。墨子は儀礼を完全に拒否するが、それを機能的効用の下に置く。聖王の成功は、精緻な儀礼ではなく、倹約的中道、务实な統治によって民の物質的福利を確保することにあった。

墨子は繰り返し堯舜禹を模範として挙げ、その主張が革新ではなく古代聖王の正道の回帰であることを示した。

## English

**Mozi "Jie Yong Zhong"** (節用中, "On Restricting Expenditure, Part II") is a foundational chapter in the [[mozicomprehensive-overview|Mozi corpus]] that articulates the Mohist doctrine of economic restraint as essential to good governance. The text presents a systematic exposition of how the ancient sage-kings achieved proper rule by restricting wasteful expenditures while ensuring the basic welfare of the people.

### Core Thesis: Sage Kings and Beneficial Governance

The chapter opens with a theoretical framework establishing why ancient sage-kings (*mingwang shengren*) achieved dominance and proper order among the feudal lords (*zheng zhuhou*). Master Mozi argues that these rulers succeeded because they possessed three essential qualities: *ai min jinshen* (愛民謹忠, loving the people and being cautious and loyal), *li min jinchou* (利民謹厚, benefiting the people with generosity), and *zhong xin xiang lian* (忠信相連, connecting loyalty with trustworthiness). Crucially, they "showed profit to the people" (*shi zhi yi li*), meaning they demonstrated how proper governance produces tangible benefits. This dual approach of moral exemplarity and material benefit resulted in ministers and subjects who were "unsatisfied throughout their lives and never weary even until death" (*zhong shen bu yan, mo shi er bu juan*).

### The Principle of Functional Division of Labor

Master Mozi then articulates the principle of appropriate allocation of social resources through the doctrine of skilled labor (*ge cong shi suo neng*, 各從其事所能). The sage-kings established a clear system where the various crafts and professions—wheelwrights (*lun che*), leatherworkers (*ji*), gourd artisans (*pao*), potters (*tao*), metalworkers (*ye*), and carpenters (*jiang zi*)—each pursued their respective callings. The governing principle is stated succinctly: "Whatever suffices for the people's use is provided; anything that adds expense without adding benefit to the people is stopped" (*fan zuoyi fenggei minyong zhu, jia fei bu jia min li ze zhi*).

### Practical Application: Food and Drink

Regarding饮食 (food and drink), Master Mozi establishes a utilitarian standard: adequate nourishment to "fill the empty stomach and continue the qi, strengthen the limbs, and make the ears and eyes keen" (*chongxu jiqi, qiang gugong, shi er mu congming*). This explicitly excludes excessive flavors (*bu ji wu wei zhi tiao*), aromatic harmony (*fenxiang zhi he*), or seeking rare and strange goods from distant lands (*zhi zhi yu gu zhen guai yi wu*).

Master Mozi illustrates this with the example of Emperor Yao's (*yaozhi tiantian*) governance: though he ruled south to Jiaozhi and north to Youdu, with territories extending to where the sun rises and sets, he maintained simplicity. His "deepest love" manifested not in elaborate cuisine but in single servings of millet and millet porridge (*zhongmi bu er, geng zhi bu chong*). He ate from earthen bowls and drank from earthen vessels, used a simple measuring cup for wine, and "the rites of deferential comportment and circling—these the sage-kings did not practice" (*fuyang zhounuan weiyi zhi li, shengwang fei wei*).

### Practical Application: Clothing

The chapter similarly restricts clothing to functional requirements: winter garments of dark blue and black (*gangzhou zhi yi*) should be light and warm; summer garments of fine hemp (*chixi zhi yi*) should be light and cool. Anything that "adds expense without adding benefit to the people" is categorically rejected by the sage-kings.

### Practical Application: Military Equipment

Master Mozi addresses defense through the lens of frugality, arguing that weapons development should serve practical protection rather than offensive aggression. He describes how ancient sage-kings, confronted with dangerous beasts and predatory people who harmed the populace (*meng qin jia shou baoren haimin*), trained the people in military discipline. The sword (*jian*) is evaluated for its function: it can penetrate when thrust and sever when struck, damaging enemies without breaking. Armor (*jia*) is praised for being light yet strong, enabling movement while providing protection. Chariots (*che*) are valued for their ability to carry heavy loads over great distances with safety and speed.

For water transport, the sage-kings recognized that great rivers and deep valleys could not be crossed by land, so they developed boats and oars. These watercraft were sufficient for passage regardless of the passenger's status—even the highest ministers and lords would use the same simple vessels, and the boatmen required no special ornamentation.

### Simple Burial Practices

The chapter prescribes the "法" (standard) of simple burial: three garments sufficient to cover the decaying flesh, a coffin three inches thick to contain the decomposing bones, burial pits deep enough to prevent access to springs and prevent noxious vapors from escaping, and the principle that once the deceased is buried, the living should not maintain prolonged mourning (*shengzhe jiu sang yong ai*).

Master Mozi grounds this in anthropological history: when humans first arose there were no houses, so they dwelt in caves dug into hillsides (*yin lingqiu ju xue er chu*). The sage-kings recognized the vulnerabilities of cave dwelling—wind and cold in winter, dampness and scorching heat from below and above in summer—and built houses accordingly. The standard for proper housing follows the same principle of restriction: structures should provide wind and cold protection, shield against snow frost and rain and dew, maintain internal cleanliness for sacrificial offerings, and provide adequate walls for male-female separation. Anything adding expense without adding benefit is rejected.

### Key Doctrines and Philosophy

**The Utility Principle (功利主義)**: The chapter represents a coherent utilitarian ethic, evaluating all practices by whether they produce net benefit. Master Mozi consistently applies the test: *jia fei bu jia min li ze zhi* (adds expense without adding benefit is stopped).

**Anti-Ceremonialism**: The text explicitly rejects elaborate ritual practices that characterized [[confucian-political-philosophy|Confucian ceremonial traditions]]. The sage-kings "did not practice" (*fei wei*) the complex rites of deferential comportment, suggesting that such practices represent wasteful expenditure rather than genuine governance.

**Historical Empiricism**: Master Mozi grounds his arguments in historical examples—Yao, Shun, and Yu—to demonstrate that the greatest ancient rulers achieved order through simplicity rather than extravagance.

**Anti-Excess**: Throughout, the chapter demonstrates that sufficiency (*zuoyi*) defines the proper measure of expenditure, distinguishing legitimate need from harmful desire.

### Historical Significance

This chapter articulates the practical wing of Mohist thought, complementing the ethical emphasis on [[jian-ai|universal love]] (*jian'ai*) with concrete policy recommendations. The "節用" chapters (上中下, upper, middle, lower) represent an important source for understanding how Mohism proposed to address social chaos—not through metaphysical cultivation alone but through material sufficiency and equitable distribution.

The text also provides valuable evidence for Warring States period economic conditions and the material concerns that animated political philosophy during this transformative era of Chinese civilization.

## 中文

**《墨子·節用中》**是墨家思想体系中论述节制用度、崇尚实用政治哲学的核心篇章。本章系统阐述了圣王通过限制奢侈消费、实现民众福祉而达致天下大治的政治主张，代表了先秦时期独特的功利主义伦理倾向。

### 核心理論：聖王成功之道

本章开篇即建立理论框架，阐明古之圣王之所以能够"王天下、正诸侯"的根本原因。墨子指出，这些圣王具备三重品质：其一，"爱民谨忠"，即真诚地爱护百姓、行事谨慎而忠诚；其二，"利民谨厚"，即慷慨地施利于民；其三，"忠信相连"，即忠诚与信任相互贯通。更重要的是，圣王能够"示之以利"，向民众展示遵循正道所带来的实际利益。这种道德示范与物质利益并行的双重路径，使得"臣民终身不餍，歿世而不卷"。

### 功能分工原則

墨子继而提出社会资源合理配置的功能分工学说。圣王确立制度，使天下各行各业——轮舆工匠、车乘匠人、皮具匠人、匏瓜工匠、陶工、冶金匠人、木梓匠人等——"各从事其所能"。其治理原则简洁明了："凡足以奉给民用诸，加费不加民利则止。"即凡能够满足民众基本需要的支出应予以保障，而那些徒增费用却不增加民众福祉的开支则应停止。

### 饮食节制

关于饮食之"法"，墨子确立功利主义标准：饮食只需"足以充虚继气，强股肱，使耳目聪明"即可。这明确排除了"五味之调、芬芳之和"等美食追求，以及"远国珍奇怪物"的搜求。

墨子以尧帝为例加以说明：尽管其治天下"南抚交阯，北降幽都，东西至日所出入，莫不宾服"，却保持着极为简朴的生活方式。"逮至其厚爱"的具体表现，并非珍馐美味，而是不变的黍粟饭菜、不重复的羹胾。帝尧"饭于土塯，啜于土形，斗以酌"，使用简陋的陶土器具，并以简朴的量器斟酒。对于"俯仰周旋威仪之礼"，墨子明确断言"圣王弗为"——圣王不从事此类繁复的礼仪形式。

### 服饰节制

本章对服饰的要求同样以功能为标准：冬服"绀緅之衣"，应"轻且暖"；夏服"絺绤之衣"，应"轻且凊"。凡"加费不加于民利者，圣王弗为"。这一原则将奢侈服饰视为不当支出，与儒家对礼制服饰的重视形成鲜明对比。

### 军事装备

墨子从节用角度论述国防：古者圣人因"猛禽狡兽暴人害民"之患，"于是教民以兵行"。对兵器、铠甲、战车的评价均以实用功能为标准：剑应能"刺则入，击则断，旁击而不折"；铠甲应"轻且利，动则兵且从"；车乘应能"服重致远"，行驶安全、引挽便利，以实现"安以不伤人，利以速至"的目标。

关于水上交通，墨子指出圣王因"大川广谷之不可济"而"利为舟楫"，其功能标准是"足以将之则上"，且"虽上者三公诸侯至，舟楫不易，津人不饰"——即便是最高统治者亦使用相同简朴之舟船，渡口吏人不需特殊装饰。

### 薄葬之法

本章规定节葬之"法"："衣三领，足以朽肉；棺三寸，足以朽骸。堀穴深不通于泉，流不发泄，则止。死者既葬，生者毋久丧用哀。"墨子将此原则追溯至人类早期历史："古者人之始生未有宫室之时，因陵丘堀穴而处焉"——穴居时代本无厚葬之习，简化葬仪不过是对本初生活方式的复归。

### 宫室之法

关于建筑标准，墨子首先追溯宫室起源：古之圣王认识到穴居的缺陷——"冬可以辟风寒"但"逮夏，下润湿，上熏烝，恐伤民之气"，故"作为宫室而利"。其建筑标准为："其旁可以圉风寒，上可以圉雪霜雨露，其中蠲洁，可以祭祀，宫墙足以为男女之别，则止诸。"功能完备即可，"加费不加民利者，圣王弗为"。

### 思想要義

**功利原则**：本章体现了系统的功利主义伦理观。墨子以"加费不加民利则止"作为评判一切实践的终极标准，具有鲜明的效益主义特征。

**反礼仪主义**：本章明确拒绝[[confucian-political-philosophy|儒家]]所重视的繁复礼仪。"俯仰周旋威仪之礼，圣王弗为"一语，将仪式性支出界定为不当浪费，与墨家重实质轻形式的思想倾向一致。

**历史经验主义**：墨子以尧舜禹等历史圣王为证，说明上古盛世源于简朴而非奢侈，具有以史为鉴的方法论意义。

**适度原则**：全篇以"足以"界定支出的合理边界，强调"足用"（sufficiency）而非"丰余"（abundance），体现了墨家独特的经济伦理思想。

### 學術意義

《節用中》与《節用上》《節用下》共同构成墨家节用学说的完整体系，是理解墨家"兼爱"思想实践维度的关键文本。本章所呈现的功利主义伦理、实用主义政治哲学，以及对形式主义[[confucian-ethics|儒家礼乐文化]]的批判，构成了先秦百家争鸣中独特而重要的思想流派。

## 日本語

**『墨子』第二十篇「節用中」**は、恵用（費用節約）と実利主義に基づく統治原理を論じた墨家の重要篇章である。本篇は、古代の聖王が奢侈な支出を制限しつつ民衆の基本福祉を確保することで天下を正しく統治したことを系統的に論じ、先秦時代における独特の功利主義倫理の展開を示すものである。

### 核心論点：聖王の成功原理

本篇は、古代の聖王が天下を王らし、諸侯を正することができた根本原因について理論的枠組みを提示することから始まる。墨子はこれらの聖王が三つの本質的資質を備えていたと論じる。第一に「愛民謹忠」——民衆を真に愛し、慎重かつ忠実であること。第二に「利民謹厚」——民衆に慷慨的に利益を施すこと。第三に「忠信相連」——忠誠と信頼が相互に贯通すること。重要的是、聖王は民衆に「利」を示し、正しい統治が具体的な利益をもたらすことを民衆に示した点である。

### 機能的分業原則

墨子は社会資源の合理的配分について機能的分業学説を提起する。聖王は制度を確立し、車輪職人、皮職人、葫芦職人、陶工、金属職人起大工などが各自的技能に応じて業務に従事することを定めた。その統治原則は簡潔に言われる。「凡そ民用に奉給するものは凡そ足るを加う、民利を加えなければ則ち已む」と。民的必要を満たす支出は保障されるべきであり、民衆の福利を増加しない支出は停止されるべきである。

### 飲食の規制

飲食について、墨子は功利主義的基準を確立する：飲食は「空虚を満たし気を継ぎ、四肢を強め、耳目を聡明にするに足る」だけで十分である。これは「五味の調和」「芳香の調和」や「遠国からの珍奇な物品」の追求を明確に除外する。

堯帝の例を挙げて墨子はこれを説明する。天下を治めた堯帝は「南は交阯撫で、北は幽都而降ろし、東西は日の出入る所に至り」、広く支配したが、簡素な生活方式を維持した。深い愛の表現は贅沢な料理ではなく、単一の粟料理と羹であり、土器から饭吃もり、土器から飲み物を吸い、小さな升で酒を計っていた。「俯仰周旋の威儀の礼」については、墨子は明確に「聖王は為さず」と断言する。

### 衣服の規制

衣服についても同様に機能を基準とする。冬の衣服は「紺緅の衣」で「軽く且つ温かい」こと、夏の衣服は「絺綌の衣」で「軽く且つ涼しい」ことを要求する。「費用を加え民利を加えなければ、聖王は為さず」という原則は、奢侈な衣服を不当な支出とみなす。

### 軍事装備

墨子は費用節約の観点から国防を論じる。古代の聖王は「猛禽狡獣暴人が民を害す」という脅威に直面し、「于是民に兵を教える」。剣は「刺さば入り、撃てば断ち、横撃しても折れず」、鎧は「軽く且つ利にして、動けば兵かつ従う」、車は「重を服し遠くに致り、乗れば安上がり、引けば利あり、安以て人を傷つけず、利以て速やかに至る」ことを基準として評価される。

### 簡葬の法

埋葬の基準を規定する：「衣三領、足以朽肉す；棺三寸、足以朽骸す。堀穴は深く泉に通ぜず、流を発洩せねば已む。死者は既葬すれば、生者は久しく丧を用うる毋かれ」。墨子はこれを人類の早期歴史に遡及する：人間の始まりには宮室がなく、「陵丘堀穴にして處んだ」——洞窟埋葬の時期に厚葬の慣習は存在しなかった。

### 思想的意义

**功利原則**：本篇は体系的な功利主義倫理を体現する。「加費不加民利則已む」という原則は、すべての実践を評価する究極的基準として機能する。

**反礼儀主義**：本篇は儒家の複雑な礼儀を明確に拒否する。「俯仰周旋の威儀の礼、聖王は為さず」という言明は、儀式的支出を浪費と界定する。

**歴史的经验主義**：墨子は堯舜禹などの歴史的聖王を例として、古代の繁栄が奢侈ではなく簡素によって達成されたことを示す。
