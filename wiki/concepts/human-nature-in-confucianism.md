## English

### Overview
In Confucian thought, **human nature** (Chinese: 性, *xìng*) is the innate disposition that underlies human conduct. While the term appears sparingly in the *Analects*, its systematic exploration became a cornerstone of later Confucian discourse, shaping debates over moral ontology, self‑cultivation, and social order.

### Classical Foundations
**Confucius (Kongzi, 551–479 BCE)** treated *xing* as a potential for moral development rather than a fixed metaphysical substance. The *Analects* (17.2) notes that “by nature, people are alike; by practice, they diverge,” implying that moral perfection is attainable through habit and learning.

### Mencius: The Goodness of *Xing*
**Mencius (Mengzi, 372–289 BCE)** argued that human nature is fundamentally good, possessing four innate moral sprouts (四端): *ren* (benevolence), *yi* (righteousness), *li* (propriety), and *zhi* (wisdom). He illustrated this with the example of water flowing downhill—human beings naturally incline toward virtue, and proper nurturing allows these sprouts to flourish into full virtues.

### Xunzi: The Evil Tendency
**Xunzi (Xunzi, c. 310–c. 237 BCE)** offered a counter‑view, contending that human nature is inclined toward selfish desires and thus “evil.” Moral order, according to Xunzi, must be imposed through *li* (ritual propriety) and *fa* (legal norms). Education and rites are indispensable for reshaping the raw inclinations of *xing*.

### Neo‑Confucian Synthesis
During the Song (960–1279) and Ming (1368–1644) dynasties, scholars such as **Zhu Xi** and **Wang Yangming** re‑interpreted *xing* in terms of *li* (principle) and *qi* (material force). Zhu Xi maintained that every person possesses the pure *li* of human nature, which can be obscured by *qi*; purification occurs through the “investigation of things” (格物致知). Wang Yangming, by contrast, emphasized the innate moral consciousness (*liangzhi*) as the direct expression of *xing*, arguing that moral action is a spontaneous unfolding of the mind.

### *Xing* and the Mandate of Heaven
Confucian cosmology links *xing* to *tian* (Heaven). The *Zhongyong* states that “the nature of the heavens is *cheng* (sincere), and the way of man follows *cheng*.” This suggests that cultivating one’s *xing* aligns individual moral agency with the cosmic order, a theme echoed in the *Daxue* (“Great Learning”) where self‑cultivation leads to the ordering of the world.

### Implications for Moral Cultivation
- **Self‑Cultivation**: Understanding *xing* determines the methods of moral development—whether through nurturing innate sprouts (Mencius) or through rigorous ritual discipline (Xunzi).
- **Social Harmony**: The Confucian belief that a well‑cultivated nature fosters benevolent governance underpins the ideal of a harmonious society.
- **Educational Policy**: Confucian curricula traditionally emphasize the cultivation of *xing* through classics, rites, and reflective practice.

## 中文

### 概述
在儒家思想中，**人性**（简称“性”）指的是人与生俱来的道德倾向与本性。尽管《论语》中对“性”提及不多，但后世儒家对它的系统阐释成为道德本体论、自我修养和社会秩序讨论的核心。

### 古典根基
**孔子**（公元前551‑前479）并未将“性”视为固定不变的本体，而是强调它是可以通过学习和实践得到完善的潜在能力。《论语·阳货》记载：“性相近，习相远”，意指人性本相似，后天的修养导致差异。
