## Additional Source: 大堅固婆羅門緣起經

  The *Discourse on the Origin of the Great Firm Brahman* (佛說大堅固婆羅門緣起經) provides a detailed narrative of renunciation that complements the philosophical framework established in the main article.

  **Stages of the Great Firm Brahmin's Renunciation:**

  The text presents a remarkably detailed account of the protagonist's gradual renunciation process, demonstrating Buddhist teachings on the hierarchy of spiritual values:

  1. **Renunciation before the King** (辭別國王): The Great Firm Brahmin first sought permission from King Linu, who initially refused, offering instead to fulfill any material need. The protagonist rejected all offers, citing his hearing of the "true words" from Mahābrahmā about abandoning defilements.

  2. **Renunciation before the Six Kings** (辭別六王): When the six companion-kings attempted to retain him with wealth (vast treasures and luxuries), he rejected these offerings, stating: "I already have abundant wealth, earned through proper means. If I abandon even my own possessions, how much more would I reject gifts from kings?"

  3. **Renunciation before the Court Brahmins** (辭別萬四千婆羅門): When 14,000 Brahmin disciples attempted to dissuade him, claiming that householders have greater benefits, he corrected them: "Those who have left home have great benefit, great authority, and great fame. Householders have minimal benefit, minimal authority, and minimal fame. All your knowledge comes from teachers." They accepted his reasoning and followed his example.

  4. **Renunciation before Forty Wives** (辭別四十妻): When the six kings offered beautiful courtesans, the protagonist responded that he already possessed forty wives of equal beauty and virtue, yet he abandoned even them. The wives supported his decision: "As you were our teacher, our husband, and our friend, we too have our proper refuge (帰趣)."

  5. **Final Seven-Day Waiting Period** (七日延引): When the kings attempted various delays (seven years, six years, down to seven days), the protagonist agreed only to the seven-day waiting period, accepting this as reasonable.

  **Aftermath of Renunciation:**

  The text emphasizes the transformative power of his example: "Within seven days, with right faith firmly established, [he] took refuge in the Buddha, his hair fell away, he donned monastic robes, and achieved the bhikkhu form with complete deportment." Following his ordination, the six kings abandoned their kingdoms and became monks, along with seven thousand Brahmin scholars, his forty wives, and countless laypeople. The Great Firm Brahmin then attained arhatship and taught the Dharma extensively.

  **Key Contrast Between Household and Renunciation:**

  The sutra articulates a clear hierarchy of spiritual value: "If I remain at home, I am constantly bound. If I leave home, I am constantly liberated. I should cultivate pure and spotless conduct. All that is born is extinguished; human life is short. If one does not awaken, [death leads to] the three unfortunate realms."
