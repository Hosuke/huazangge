## English

### Definition
In Buddhist doctrine the **three roots of unwholesomeness** (Pali: *akusala‑mūla*; Sanskrit: *akuśala‑mūla*) are the three basic defilements that generate all unwholesome states of mind and their corresponding actions. They are:

1. **Lobha** – greed, desire, attachment; the wish to possess or cling to people, objects, or ideas.
2. **Dosa** – hatred, aversion, ill‑will; the opposite of loving‑kindness, manifesting as anger, irritation, or fear.
3. **Moha** – delusion, ignorance; the fundamental misunderstanding of the nature of reality, especially the non‑self (anattā) and impermanence (anicca).

These three are called “roots” because they are the underlying sources from which the whole tree of unwholesome mental factors (e.g., envy, jealousy, pride, doubt) grows.

### Role in Buddhist Theory
- **Karmic Generation**: According to the law of kamma, actions motivated by these roots produce unwholesome fruits (unhappy rebirth, suffering). Conversely, actions arising from their wholesome counterparts (alobha, adosa, amoha) generate merit and lead to liberation.
- **Mental Continuity**: In the Abhidharma analysis of consciousness, lobha, dosa, and moha are listed among the 14 unwholesome mental factors that can accompany any moment of consciousness (cittas).
- **Four Right Efforts**: The Buddha’s teaching on the four right efforts (sammappadhāna) includes the prevention of arising, the overcoming, the cultivation, and the maintenance of wholesome states precisely to counteract these three roots.

### Relation to the Three Wholesome Roots
| Unwholesome Root | Wholesome Counterpart | Positive Quality |
|------------------|-----------------------|------------------|
| Lobha           | Alobha               | Generosity, non‑attachment |
| Dosa            | Adosa                | Loving‑kindness, compassion |
| Moha            | Amoha                | Wisdom, clear understanding |

### Scriptural Sources
- **Pali Canon**: The *Aṅguttara Nikāya* (AN 3.16) and *Saṃyutta Nikāya* (SN 45.172) list the three as the “three akusala‑mūlāni.”
- **Mahāyāna Texts**: The *Mahāyāna Avataṃsaka Sūtra* and the *Abhidharma‑kośa* (by Vasubandhi) treat them as fundamental to both Theravāda and Mahāyāna analysis of mind.
- **Chinese Āgamas**: The *Saṃyuktāgama* (《杂阿含经》) and the *Madhyamāgama* (《中阿含经》) contain parallel passages.

### Manifestations
- **Lobha‑derived factors**: covetousness, miserliness, sensual lust.
- **Dosa‑derived factors**: anger, revenge, cruelty, jealousy.
- **Moha‑derived factors**: ignorance, doubt, wrong view, confusion about causality.

1. **Ethical Reflection (sati‑sampajañña)**: Practitioners examine whether their motives stem from lobha, dosa, or moha before acting.
