Paragraph 1: Overview: The Doctrine of the Mean, also known as Zhongyong, is one of the Four Books of Confucianism, traditionally attributed to Zisi (Kong Ji), the grandson of Confucius, and recorded by his disciple Gong Xi. It was later incorporated into the Confucian canon by Zhu Xi in the Song dynasty as part of the Four Books.

Paragraph 2: Core ideas: The term "zhong" (中) means "middle," "balanced," or "central," and "yong" (庸) can be translated as "constancy," "ordinary," or "use." The text argues that the true moral path lies in following the "mean"—the balanced way between extremes. This principle of the mean is not a passive mediocrity but an active, dynamic equilibrium that aligns human behavior with the order of the cosmos.

Paragraph 3: Sincerity (cheng, 誠) is the foundation of the mean. The text declares: "Cheng is what completes the self; the path is what directs the self." Sincerity is both an inner state and a metaphysical principle: "If sincerity is present, then the thing is; if sincerity is absent, the thing perishes." Through sincere self-cultivation, the individual can achieve "central harmony" (zhonghe, 中和), whereby the inner emotions (pleasure, anger, sorrow, fear) are properly regulated.

Paragraph 4: The practice of the mean involves "watchfulness over oneself when alone" (shendu, 慎獨). This self‑vigilance ensures that moral rectitude is maintained even in private, preventing the distortion of emotions and actions. The exemplary person (junzi, 君子) embodies the mean in everyday life, acting in accordance with theDao while remaining responsive to particular circumstances.

Paragraph 5: Metaphysical dimension: Zhongyong connects personal morality with cosmic order. It states: "When the emotions are harmonized, the heavens and earth attain their proper order, and all things flourish." This reflects the Confucian ideal of "tian ren he yi" (天人合一), the unity of humanity and the cosmos.

Paragraph 6: Influence: The work became a central text in Neo‑Confucianism, especially in Zhu Xi's commentary, which emphasized the importance of "investigation of things" (gewu) and "self‑cultivation" (xiushen). It shaped the civil‑service examination curriculum for centuries, and its ideas were transmitted to Korea and Japan, influencing the Silhak school in Korea and the Edo‑period Confucian scholars such as Hayashi Razan.

Paragraph 7: Modern reception: In contemporary scholarship, Zhongyong is studied for its insights into moral psychology, the concept of equilibrium, and its relevance to discussions of balance in ethics, education, and environmental philosophy.
