## English

### Overview
**Confucian political philosophy** (儒家政治哲學) is the branch of Confucian thought that examines the moral foundations of political authority, the responsibilities of rulers and officials, and the ways in which a just society can be realized through the cultivation of virtue. Originating in the teachings of Confucius (孔子) and further elaborated by his successors—notably Mencius (孟子) and Xunzi (荀子)—the tradition has profoundly shaped Chinese imperial ideology, later Neo‑Confucianism, and contemporary East Asian discussions on governance.

### Historical Development
1. **Pre‑Qin Foundations (6th–3rd c. BCE)** – Confucius’s *Analects* emphasize *ren* (benevolence), *li* (ritual), and the model of the *junzi* (gentleman/ruler). Mencius expands these ideas with the theory of innate moral goodness and the ideal of the “sagely king.”
2. **Han Dynasty Synthesis (2nd c. BCE–2nd c. CE)** – The Han emperor Wu adopts Confucianism as state orthodoxy; the *Mandate of Heaven* becomes a legitimizing principle for dynastic rule.
3. **Tang‑Song Institutionalization (7th–13th c.)** – The civil‑service examination system is refined; Neo‑Confucian scholars such as Zhu Xi codify the *Four Books* as canonical texts for political education.
4. **Ming‑Qing Consolidation (14th–19th c.)** – Neo‑Confucianism remains the elite worldview; scholars debate the balance between moral cultivation and administrative efficiency.
5. **Modern Reinterpretations (20th c. –present)** – Intellectuals re‑examine Confucian political ideas in light of democracy, human rights, and globalization, seeking “Confucian democracy” and “civil society” models.

### Core Principles
- **Ren (仁)** – Benevolence; the ruler’s heartfelt concern for the welfare of the people.
- **Li (礼)** – Ritual propriety; codified behaviours that sustain social harmony.
- **Yi (义)** – Righteousness; the moral judgment that guides political decisions.
