## English
The Analects of Confucius, known as 论语 (lún yǔ) in Chinese, is a compilation of sayings and ideas attributed to the Chinese philosopher Confucius (Kong Fuzi). The text is one of the foundational works of Confucianism and emphasizes morality, societal relationships, and personal conduct.

### Key Themes
- **Moral Virtue**: Confucius advocates for the importance of virtue, suggesting that individuals must practice benevolence and righteousness in their daily lives.
- **Respect for Tradition**: In the Analects, Confucius stresses the significance of historical precedence, particularly the relevance of the Zhou dynasty as a model for governance.
- **Importance of Ritual**: The text discusses the critical role of ritual (礼, lǐ) in cultivating moral integrity and social order, implying that adherence to ritual promotes harmony in society.

### Selected Passages
1. **礼之用，和为贵**: "The use of ritual should promote harmony" highlights the essence of social interactions.
2. **仁者安仁，知者利仁**: A reflection on the relationship between knowledge and moral conduct, indicating that those who embody benevolence are at peace with themselves.

### Cultural Impact
The teachings of Confucius have significantly influenced Chinese culture and governance, stressing the moral responsibilities of rulers and the significance of education and self-improvement.

## English
The Analects of Confucius, known in Chinese as the 论语 (Lunyu), is a collection of phrases and ideas attributed to the Chinese philosopher Confucius and his disciples. It is an essential text of Confucian philosophy, covering various topics such as morality, politics, education, and ethics. 

### Key Teachings
1. **Learning and Teaching**: Confucius placed a strong emphasis on the importance of education and continuous learning. He is quoted as saying, "Learning without thought is labor lost; thought without learning is perilous." This highlights the balance between knowledge acquisition and critical thinking.

2. **Moral Integrity**: The concept of ren (仁), often translated as "benevolence" or "humaneness," is central to Confucian ethics. Confucius believed that true moral character stems from a genuine concern for others and the practice of virtue.

3. **Role of the Junzi**: Confucius described the junzi (君子), or "gentleman," as a person who embodies moral integrity and ethical behavior. The junzi strives for self-improvement and exhibits virtues such as righteousness, propriety, and wisdom.

4. **Political Philosophy**: Confucius emphasized the role of ethical governance and the moral authority of rulers. He argued that leaders should govern by virtue and lead by example, thus earning the respect and loyalty of their subjects.

5. **Social Relationships**: Confucianism stresses the importance of hierarchical relationships, especially within the family, characterized by filial piety and respect for elders.

### Conclusion
The teachings in The Analects have had a profound influence not only in China but also across Asia, shaping moral and political thought for centuries. They present a vision of a society that values education, ethical governance, and the cultivation of personal virtues.

### Overview
The Analects of Confucius is a collection of sayings and ideas attributed to the Chinese philosopher Confucius and his contemporaries. It forms the foundation of Confucianism and addresses themes such as morality, ethics, and the importance of proper conduct.

### Key Concepts
#### Military Training
Confucius emphasizes the necessity of training citizens in the art of warfare:
> "It is said that if a good man teaches the people for seven years, he can train them to fight." *(Analects 13.30)*

He argues against sending untrained men to war, which he equates to abandoning them.

#### Righteousness and Virtue
Confucius discusses various qualities that characterize a gentleman:
> "A gentleman may be unkind, but there are none amongst the small people who are kind." *(Analects 14.6)*

He also mentions:
> "Those who have virtue must have words; those who have words may not necessarily have virtue." *(Analects 14.4)*

This underpins the distinction between moral integrity and the ability to communicate effectively.

#### Importance of Knowledge
Confucius stresses the significance of continuous learning and self-refinement:
> "The scholars of ancient times learned for themselves, today’s scholars learn for others." *(Analects 14.24)*

This reflects the transition from learning for personal development to learning for external validation.

### Summary
The Analects encapsulate Confucian ideals, laying emphasis on ethics, governance, and personal conduct. It remains a vital resource for understanding Chinese philosophy and culture.

## English
The *Analects of Confucius* are a collection of sayings and ideas attributed to the Chinese philosopher Confucius and his disciples, emphasizing moral integrity, proper governance, and the cultivation of virtue. This text serves as a foundation for Confucian thought and practice. 

### Key Concepts
1. **Governance by Virtue**: Confucius emphasizes that rulers should adhere to ritual and propriety. He states, "When rulers uphold rites, the people are easier to govern" (14.42).
   
2. **The Junzi (君子)**: This concept, often translated as "gentleman" or "noble person," highlights a moral ideal in Confucian philosophy. Confucius describes the junzi as someone who cultivates themselves to ensure the tranquility of both the elite and common people (14.42).

3. **Self-Cultivation**: The notion of self-improvement is paramount in Confucianism. A common question posed to Confucius is about the characteristics of the junzi, to which he replies consistently that self-cultivation is essential (14.43).

4. **Respect and Duty**: The *Analects* extol the importance of respect for elders and maintaining proper relationships, reflecting broader societal values that contribute to social harmony. For example, when assessing a young boy's behavior, Confucius notes that lack of respect for elders indicates a lack of proper upbringing (14.44).

5. **Education**: Confucius advocates for education as a means to cultivate virtue and prepare for responsible citizenship. He believes in teaching without discrimination, regardless of social class (15.39).

6. **Moral Decision Making**: The text outlines how to navigate moral dilemmas, often emphasizing the significance of intention. The principle "What you do not wish for yourself, do not do to others" is proposed as a guiding rule for ethical conduct (15.24).

### Conclusion
The *Analects* encapsulate a significant portion of Confucian thought, focusing on morality, ethics, and the social obligations that come with learnedness and leadership.

## English
The Jishi chapter of the [[confucius-analects]] contains some of Confucius' most profound teachings on political philosophy, personal virtue, and social relationships. This chapter addresses the proper exercise of power, the cultivation of character, and the importance of education in creating a harmonious society.

### Political Philosophy and Governance
Confucius begins by criticizing the Ji family's plan to attack Zhuanyu, arguing that proper governance should focus on internal virtue rather than external expansion. He famously states: "I have heard that rulers of states and chiefs of families are not troubled lest their people should be few, but are troubled lest they should not keep their several places; that they are not troubled with fears of poverty, but are troubled with fears of a want of contented repose among the people in their several places. For when the people keep their several places, there will be no poverty; when harmony prevails, there will be no scarcity of people; and when there is such a contented repose, there will be no rebellious upsettings."

Confucius further elaborates on the proper source of authority: "When right principles prevail in the kingdom, ceremonies, music, and punitive military expeditions proceed from the son of Heaven. When right principles do not prevail in the kingdom, ceremonies, music, and punitive military expeditions proceed from the princes."

### Three Friends, Three Pleasures, and Three Errors
Confucius identifies three types of beneficial friends and three types of harmful friends: "There are three friendships which are advantageous, and three which are injurious. Friendship with the upright; friendship with the sincere; and friendship with the man of much observation: these are advantageous. Friendship with the man of specious airs; friendship with the insinuatingly soft; and friendship with the glib-tongued: these are injurious."

Similarly, he discusses three beneficial pleasures and three harmful pleasures: "There are three things men find enjoyment in which are advantageous, and three things they find enjoyment in which are injurious. To find enjoyment in the discriminating study of ceremonies and music; to find enjoyment in speaking of the goodness of others; to find enjoyment in having many worthy friends: these are advantageous. To find enjoyment in extravagant pleasures; to find enjoyment in idleness and sauntering; to find enjoyment in the pleasures of feasting: these are injurious."

Regarding conversation with superiors, Confucius warns of three errors: "There are three errors to which they who stand in the presence of a man of virtue and station are liable. They may speak when it does not come to them to speak—this is called rashness. They may not speak when it comes to them to speak—this is called concealment. They may speak without looking at the countenance of their superior—this is called blindness."

### Three Cautions, Three Reverences, and Nine Considerations
Confucius outlines three stages of life requiring different cautions: "There are three things which the [[junzi]] guards against. In youth, when the physical powers are not yet settled, he guards against lust. When he is strong and the physical powers are full of vigor, he guards against quarrelsomeness. When he is old, and the animal powers are decayed, he guards against covetousness."

He also identifies three objects of reverence: "There are three things of which the [[junzi]] stands in awe. He stands in awe of the ordinances of Heaven. He stands in awe of great men. He stands in awe of the words of sages."

The nine considerations represent a comprehensive guide to ethical conduct: "The [[junzi]] has nine things which are subjects with him of thoughtful consideration. In regard to the use of his eyes, he is anxious to see clearly. In regard to the use of his ears, he is anxious to hear distinctly. In regard to his countenance, he is anxious that it should be benign. In regard to his demeanor, he is anxious that it should be respectful. In regard to his speech, he is anxious that it should be sincere. In regard to his doing of business, he is anxious that it should be reverently careful. In regard to what he doubts about, he is anxious to question others. When he is angry, he thinks of the difficulties his anger may involve him in. When he sees gain to be got, he thinks of righteousness."

### Educational Philosophy
Confucius emphasizes the importance of learning through his conversation with his son: "If you don't study the Odes, you won't know how to speak. If you don't study the rites, you won't know how to take your stand." This demonstrates his belief that education in poetry and ritual forms the foundation of proper conduct and social integration.

孔子开篇即批评季氏欲伐颛臾的计划，提出"不患寡而患不均，不患贫而患不安"的重要政治原则。他强调："盖均无贫，和无寡，安无倾"，主张通过修文德来吸引远方之人，而非使用武力征伐。

关于权力正当性，孔子提出："天下有道，则礼乐征伐自天子出；天下无道，则礼乐征伐自诸侯出"。这一论述确立了儒家关于政治秩序合法性的基本标准，强调权力应当集中于天子，以维护社会和谐。

### 交友之道与言行规范
孔子提出"益者三友，损者三友"的著名论断："友直，友谅，友多闻，益矣。友便辟，友善柔，友便佞，损矣"。这一区分体现了儒家对人际关系道德品质的重视。

关于侍奉君子的言行规范，孔子指出三种过失："言未及之而言谓之躁，言及之而不言谓之隐，未见颜色而言谓之瞽"。这反映了儒家对人际交往中时机把握与察言观色的重视。

### 人生修养与道德实践
孔子提出"君子三戒"的人生指导："少之时，血气未定，戒之在色；及其壮也，血气方刚，戒之在鬭；及其老也，血气既衰，戒之在得"。这一论述体现了儒家对不同年龄阶段人性弱点的深刻洞察。

"君子九思"则提供了全面的道德实践指南："视思明，听思聪，色思温，貌思恭，言思忠，事思敬，疑思问，忿思难，见得思义"。这九项考虑涵盖了感官、言行、情绪等各个方面，构成了完整的道德修养体系。

### 教育理念与学习之道
通过"陈亢问于伯鱼"的对话，孔子强调了《诗》与《礼》在人格养成中的基础地位："不学诗，无以言"、"不学礼，无以立"。这一教育理念体现了儒家对文化传承与道德教育的并重。

## 日本語
『論語』季氏篇は、孔子の政治哲学、個人の徳性、社会的関係に関する最も深遠な教えを含んでいる。本章では、権力の適切な行使、人格の陶冶、調和のとれた社会を創り出すための教育の重要性が論じられている。

### 政治哲学と統治理念
孔子はまず、季氏が顓臾を攻撃する計画を批判し、適切な統治は外部への拡大ではなく内部の徳性に焦点を当てるべきだと論じる。彼は有名な言葉で「寡なきを患えずして均しからざるを患え、貧しきを患えずして安からざるを患う」と述べ、武力ではなく文徳を修めることで遠方の人々を引き寄せるべきだと主張する。

権力の正当性について、孔子は「天下道あるときは、礼楽征伐天子より出ず。天下道なきときは、礼楽征伐諸侯より出ず」と述べ、社会の調和を維持するために権力は天子に集中すべきだという儒家的政治秩序の基本基準を確立した。

### 交友関係と言行の規範
孔子は「益者三友、損者三友」という有名な区別を提示する：「直きを友とし、諒なるを友とし、多聞なるを友とす、益なり。便辟なるを友とし、善柔なるを友とし、便佞なるを友とす、損なり」。この区別は、儒教が人間関係における道徳的品質を重視することを示している。

君子に仕える際の言行規範について、孔子は三つの過ちを指摘する：「言未だ之に及ばずして言う、これを躁という。言之に及びて言わざる、これを隠という。未だ顔色を見ざるして言う、これを瞽という」。これは、儒教が人間交渉におけるタイミングの把握と顔色を読むことの重要性を重視することを反映している。

### 人生修養と道徳実践
孔子は「君子三戒」という人生指導を提出する：「少のとき、血気未だ定まらず、之を戒むるは色に在り。其の壮なるに及び、血気方に剛し、之を戒むるは闘いに在り。其の老いるに及び、血気既に衰う、之を戒むるは得に在り」。この論述は、儒教が異なる年齢段階における人間性の弱点に対する深い洞察を示している。

「君子九思」は包括的な道徳実践の指針を提供する：「視れば明を思い、聴けば聡を思い、色は温を思い、貌は恭を思い、言は忠を思い、事は敬を思い、疑えば問を思い、忿れば難を思い、得を見れば義を思う」。これら九つの考慮は、感覚、言行、情緒などあらゆる側面をカバーし、完全な道徳修養体系を構成している。

### 教育理念と学習の道
「陳亢伯魚に問う」の対話を通じて、孔子は『詩』と『礼』が人格形成における基礎的地位を占めることを強調する：「詩を学ばざれば、以て言うこと無し」、「礼を学ばざれば、以て立つこと無し」。この教育理念は、儒教が文化継承と道徳教育をともに重視することを示している。
