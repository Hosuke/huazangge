## English

Mozi's "Standard of Law" (法儀, Fayí) chapter opens with the fundamental premise that **nothing in the world can be accomplished without a standard or model** (無法儀而其事能成者，無有). This principle applies universally—to generals and ministers, to craftsmen of all trades, and to governance itself.

Mozi illustrates this through the example of the "five tools" (五法) that all craftsmen, skilled or unskilled, must use: the **square** (矩) for creating right angles, the **compass** (規) for circles, the **plumb line** (縣) for vertical straightness, the **water level** (衡) for horizontality, and the **hanging line** (繩) for checking straightness. Even the most skilled craftsman cannot deviate from these tools, while the unskilled must follow them precisely to accomplish their work.

The chapter then addresses the crucial question: what should serve as the standard for governance? Mozi systematically eliminates the candidates of **parents**, **teachers**, and **rulers**—not because they lack authority, but because most are not benevolent (仁). A standard derived from those who themselves lack benevolence cannot be correct. Since "法不仁，不可以為法" (a law that is not benevolent cannot serve as law), only a truly benevolent source can provide a valid standard.

Mozi concludes that only **Heaven** (天) qualifies as the ultimate standard. Heaven possesses three essential qualities: its **scope is vast and impartial** (行廣而無私), its **benefits are deep without claiming virtue** (施厚而不德), and its **brightness endures without declining** (明久而不衰). Because Heaven operates without favoritism, its standard is truly universal.

Having established Heaven as the standard, Mozi then specifies what Heaven actually wills: that people **mutually love and benefit one another** (相愛相利) rather than mutually hate and harm each other (相惡相賊). The evidence lies in Heaven's creation of all nations as "Heaven's cities" (天之邑) and all people as "Heaven's subjects" (天之臣)—the very fact that Heaven sustains all life demonstrates its desire for universal benefit.

The chapter concludes with historical examples. The **sage kings** (聖王)—Yu, Tang, Wen, and Wu—**universally loved the people**, reverenced Heaven and honored the spirits, so Heaven blessed them with prosperity and the Mandate to rule. The **tyrant kings** (暴王)—Jie, Zhòu, You, and Li—**universally harmed the people**, reviled Heaven and insulted the spirits, so Heaven cursed them with destruction and posterity's condemnation. The lesson is clear: those who practice good will be rewarded, those who do evil will be punished—Heaven's standard admits no exceptions.

This chapter establishes the foundational methodology of Mozi's political philosophy: reason (故) must be grounded in Heaven's will, and governance that aligns with Heaven's impartial love will succeed, while governance that violates it will fail.

## 中文

《墨子·法儀》篇開篇即確立一基本命題：天下之事，無不以法儀為準則，無法儀則事不成。此原理適用於一切領域，無論將相百工，皆需有所遵循。

墨子以百工之事為喻，說明法儀之必要性。凡百工作業，皆以五者為法：矩以為方，規以為圓，繩以為直，縣以為正，水以為平。此五法者，巧工與不巧工皆當遵循。巧者能中，不巧者雖不能中，亦當依此從事。此喻旨在說明：無論才能高下，標準皆不可廢。

繼而，墨子設問：治國之法，當以何者為準？對此，墨子逐一駁斥三種可能之準則：父母、學者、君王。此三者之所以不可為法，蓋因其間仁者寡而常者眾。若皆法其不仁之父母、不仁之學者、不仁之君王，則法本身已失其仁性，烏得為法？「法不仁，不可以為法」，此為墨子立論之核心邏輯。

既然父母、學、君三者皆不可法，墨子遂提出唯一之準則：**天**。天之所以可法，基於三重品質：天之行廣而無私，其施厚而不德，其明久而不衰。天之行無私，故能為普遍準則；天之施厚而不德，正見其無所偏愛；天之光耀久而不衰，則其標準恆久不易。

天既為準則，則天之所欲者何？墨子明言：天欲人相愛相利，不欲人相惡相賊。其所以知天欲兼愛兼利者，以天兼而有之、兼而食之故。天覆天下，養育萬民，此非兼愛兼利而何？由此推之，凡愛人利人者，天必福之；凡惡人賊人者，天必禍之。

最後，墨子以歷史為證。昔者聖王禹湯文武，兼愛天下之百姓，尊天事鬼，故天福之，使立為天子。暴王桀紂幽厲，兼惡天下之百姓，詬天侮鬼，故天禍之，使失其國家，身死為僇於天下。是故，愛人利人以得福者有矣，惡人賊人以得禍者亦有矣。

《法儀》篇所確立者，乃墨子政治哲學之方法論根基：以天為法，天之所欲則為之，天所不欲則止。此為墨子「天志」思想之綱領性文獻，奠定了其整個思想體系之方法論基礎。

## 日本語

『墨子·法儀』篇は、天下のいかなることを行うにも法儀（規範・標準）が不可欠であるという基本命題から始まる。無法儀にして事が成る者はいない。この原理は将相にも工匠にも適用され、万物に通じる普遍的な原則である。

墨子は工匠の例を引き合いに出し、五つの道具（矩・規・縣・衡・縄）がいかに巧拙に関わらず必要かを論じる。巧者はより高度にこれを当て、不巧者はこの基準に従って作業を続ける。この喻えは、才能の高低にかかわらず、基準なしには何も成就し得ないことを示している。

次に墨子は問う：統治の基準は誰が定めるべきか。父母・学者・君主という三つの候補を順序だって否定する。これらの大多数は仁徳の欠如しており、「法不仁、不可以為法」――仁徳を欠いた法則は法則たり得ない――という論理により、不仁なる者を基準とすることは自己矛盾的である。

天を基準と定めれば、次に問うべきは天的意思の内容である。墨子は明確に言う：天は人々が相互に愛し合い、利益を与え合うことを欲し、相互に憎み合い、傷つけ合うことを欲하지 않는다。天的意思のこの内容は、天がすべての国を「天之城」とし、すべての人民を「天之臣」と定めた事実から証明される。天がすべての生き物を养活し続けるからこそ、天は兼愛・兼利を欲するのである。

篇の結論として、墨子は歴史的事例を挙げる。禹・湯・文武の聖王たちは天下の百姓を兼愛し、天を尊び鬼を敬ったので、天はこれを祝福し天子たらしめた。他方、桀・紂・幽・厲の暴王たちは天下の百姓を兼悪し、天を辱め鬼を侮ったので、天はこれを災いして国を失わせ、子孫に譴責された。この歴史の教訓は明確である：善を行う者は天から福を受け、悪を行う者は天から禍を受ける。

『法儀』篇は墨子の政治哲学の方法論的基盤を確立する：天を基準とし、天の欲する所を知ってこれに従い、天の欲せぬ所は止める。この天志思想の綱領的文献は、墨子思想体系全体の方法論的基盤をなしている。

## English

**"Following Heaven as the Standard"** (以天為法, yǐ tiān wéi fǎ) represents one of the core methodological principles in Mozi's political and ethical philosophy. The term appears in the "Standard of Law" (法儀) chapter, where Mozi argues that since parents, teachers, and rulers cannot serve as valid standards for human conduct—because most of them lack benevolence—only Heaven can provide a truly reliable and universal standard.

The logic of this principle rests on three characteristic qualities that Heaven possesses:

First, **Heaven acts broadly without favoritism** (行廣而無私). Unlike human rulers who may favor their own families or followers, Heaven governs the entire world without partiality. This impartiality ensures that Heaven's standard applies equally to all people, regardless of their social status or relationship to any particular authority.

Second, **Heaven's benefits are deep without claiming virtue** (施厚而不德). Heaven bestows its blessings freely and generously, without expecting recognition or return. This characteristic distinguishes Heaven's standard from human standards, which often come with conditions, expectations, or self-interested motivations.

Third, **Heaven's brightness endures without declining** (明久而不衰). Unlike human standards that may change with generations or fade with the death of wise rulers, Heaven's standard is eternal and unchanging. This constancy makes Heaven the only reliable foundation for ethics and governance.

Once Heaven is established as the standard, the content of that standard becomes the next question. Mozi's answer is that Heaven desires people to **mutually love and benefit one another** (相愛相利) while avoiding mutual hatred and harm (相惡相賊). The practical application of "following Heaven as the standard" therefore means: "whatever Heaven desires, act accordingly; whatever Heaven does not desire, cease."

This principle has profound implications for governance. Rulers who follow Heaven's standard will govern in accordance with the principle of universal love (兼愛), will honor Heaven and the spirits, and will be rewarded with prosperity and the Mandate to rule. Rulers who reject Heaven's standard will act tyrannically, harm the people, and eventually face Heaven's punishment and destruction.

The concept also establishes Mozi's distinctive methodology in debates with other philosophers. When Mozi argues against the Confucian emphasis on ritual and music, or when he debates with followers of Yang Zhu or other schools, he grounds his arguments not in human precedent or authority, but in Heaven's will. This "reasoning from Heaven" (推理於天) approach gives his arguments a cosmic foundation that transcends human partiality.

## 中文

**「以天為法」**是墨子政治倫理哲學的核心方法論原則之一。此語出自《墨子·法儀》篇，墨子在此論證：既然父母、學者、君王皆不能為人事之準則——蓋此三者間仁者寡而常者眾——則唯有天能提供真正可靠且普遍之標準。

此原則之邏輯根基建於天所具有的三重品質：

其一，**天之行廣而無私**。天之運行，覆蓋天下，無有偏私，不同於人間君王往往偏愛其親屬或臣從。天之無私，確保天之準則適用於天下所有人，無論其社會地位如何，亦無論其與任何特定權威之關係如何。

其二，**天之施厚而不德**。天之恩施深厚，養育萬物，卻不以此居功，不求回報。此一特性使天之標準區別於人之標準——人制訂之標準往往附帶條件、期望或私利之動機。

其三，**天之明久而不衰**。天之光明恆久不滅，迥異於人定之標準——後者隨世代更迭而變易，或隨賢明君王之歿而衰亡。天之標準恆常不變，此恆久性使天成為倫理與治道之唯一可靠根基。

天既立為標準，則標準之內容遂為其次第當問之問題。墨子之回答是：天欲人相愛相利，不欲人相惡相賊。因此，「以天為法」之實際意涵便是：天之所欲則為之，天之所不欲則止。

此原則於治道有深遠意涵。循天之準則治國者，將行兼愛之道，尊天敬鬼，而受天之福佑，享天下之統治權。不循天之準則者，將行暴政，賊害百姓，終遭天罰而滅亡。

「以天為法」之概念亦確立墨子與他家辯論之獨特方法論。墨子反對儒家之禮樂，或與楊朱之徒辯論，皆不以人事先例或權威為據，而以天意為言。此「推理於天」之方法，使墨子之論證具有超越人間偏私的宇宙論基礎。

## English

**法儀** (Fǎ Yí), literally "Standard of Law" or "Model for Law," is a fundamental chapter from the Mozi that establishes the necessity of having a universal standard for governance and human conduct. Master Mozi opens with the principle that nothing of importance can be accomplished without a proper standard or model to follow. Even skilled artisans—whether carpenters using the carpenter's square, compass, straightedge, level, or water-balance—all rely on standardized tools and methods. Mozi argues that governance is no different: those who would govern the world or the states must also have a 法儀, a legal standard or model to follow.

The chapter systematically rejects the most obvious candidates for this standard. First, Mozi considers parents as a potential model, but rejects this because "the world has many parents, but few are benevolent." To model oneself on parents would be to model on lack of benevolence—a fundamentally flawed approach. Second, scholars and teachers are considered, yet the same objection applies: there are many scholars, but few仁 (benevolent). Third, and most surprisingly, Mozi extends this critique to rulers themselves: "the world has many rulers, but few are benevolent," making them equally unsuitable as standards for law.

Having dismissed human authorities, Mozi proposes 天 (Heaven) as the ultimate standard. The chapter articulates a sophisticated theology: Heaven acts universally without partiality (無私), bestows benefits without claiming virtue (施厚而不德), and shines eternally without decay (久而不衰). The sage kings of antiquity—Yu, Tang, Wen, and Wu—succeeded precisely because they modeled their governance on Heaven, loving all people universally (兼愛天下) and honoring Heaven through proper sacrifice. Conversely, the tyrant kings—Jie, Zhou, You, and Li—brought ruin upon themselves because they followed their own desires rather than Heaven's standard, harming rather than benefiting the people.

The crucial link is that Heaven's nature is fundamentally one of universal love and mutual benefit (相愛相利), not partiality or favoritism. Mozi deduces this from observation: Heaven provides for all people equally, regardless of their station, giving them the sun, moon, stars, wind, rain, and the harvests of the earth. Therefore, those who wish to follow Heaven must likewise love all people equally and work for their mutual benefit. The chapter concludes with a karmic principle: those who love and benefit others will receive Heaven's blessings, while those who hate and harm others will incur Heaven's disasters.

## 中文

**法儀**，即「法度」與「儀表」之合稱，乃《墨子》一書中之重要篇章，闡述治國理政必須遵循某種標準或模範之根本道理。墨子開篇即言：「天下從事者，不可以無法儀。無法儀而其事能成者，無有。」此語揭示一普遍原理：凡成就事業者，必有所依據之法則；無法則而能成功者，未之有也。

墨子首先以百工之事為喻，說明標準之必要性。百工為方以矩，為圓以規，直以繩，衡以水，正以縣——巧者不巧者，皆以此五者為法。此理推而至於治國，亦復如是。然而天下大事——治天下、治大國——竟「無法所度」，墨子對此深表批判，謂此不若百工之辯也。

法儀篇之核心論證，在於系統地否定人類權威作為標準之可能性。父母之標準不可用，蓋「天下之為父母者眾，而仁者寡」；學者之標準不可用，蓋「天下之為學者眾，而仁者寡」；君主之標準亦不可用，蓋「天下之為君者眾，而仁者寡」。墨子由此得出結論：父母、學、君三者，莫可以為治法。

既否定人為標準，墨子遂提出「莫若法天」之主張。天之行具有三重特性：其一，廣大無私；其二，施厚而不德；其三，其明久而不衰。天之無私，恰與人主之偏私形成對照；天之施厚而不德，正與聖王之功德相合；天之明久而不衰，則超越人間統治者之壽命限制。

墨子論證天之意志時，提出「兼而愛之、兼而利之」之命題。天兼有天下之土，兼食天下之人，故天必然希望人相愛相利，而非相惡相賊。此乃由天之「兼有」「兼食」之行為，推知天之「所欲」。古之聖王——禹湯文武——皆因兼愛天下百姓、尊天事鬼，而得天之福佑，使立為天子。反之，暴王——桀紂幽厲——因兼惡天下百姓、詬天侮鬼，而得天之禍患，身死為僇於天下。

**法儀**（ほうぎ）は、『墨子』における根本的な一篇であり、政治と人倫における普遍的な規範の必要性について論じたものである。墨子は言う：「天下从事者，不可以无法仪」——天下において 무엇を成就しようとする者も、法儀なくしては成功し得ないと。工人が矩で方を結び、規で円を描き、準縄で直線を引くように、治国者もまた従うべき標準を持たねばならない。

天の本質は「相愛相利」にある。天は天下を兼有し天下の民を兼食する，故に天は必然的に人に相爱相利することを欲し、人に相悪相賊することを欲しない。这就是法儀篇的天道观と兼愛思想の統合である。

## English

**The Standard of Law (法儀)** is a foundational chapter from the Mozi (墨子) text attributed to the philosopher Mozi (Mozi, c. 470–391 BCE). This chapter establishes the critical importance of having standards, measures, or normative principles (法儀) for all human endeavors—from governance to craftsmanship.

### The Necessity of Standards

The chapter opens with a categorical assertion: "In all affairs under heaven, one cannot proceed without standards." Without proper standards, no undertaking can succeed. Even military generals and ministers require standards, and all artisans possess their own: the carpenter uses the square (矩), the draftsman uses the compass (規), the straightedge uses the plumb line (繩), and the leveler uses water (水). Whether skilled or unskilled, all craftsmen rely on these five instruments as their standard. Those with skill hit the mark; those without skill, though they may miss, still follow the standard in their work.

### Critique of Human Standards

Mozi then systematically eliminates parents, teachers, and rulers as valid standards for governance. He observes that while the world has many parents, few are benevolent (仁). Therefore, following parents as a standard would constitute following an unbenevolent standard—which is unacceptable. The same logic applies to teachers and rulers: most are not benevolent, so none of these three can serve as the proper standard for governance.

### Heaven as the Ultimate Standard

The solution, according to Mozi, is to take Heaven (天) as the standard. Heaven's characteristics make it uniquely qualified: it acts broadly without selfish bias (行廣而無私), bestows generosity without seeking recognition (施厚而不德), and its brightness does not diminish (明久而不衰). The sage-kings took Heaven as their model.

### Heaven's Will: Mutual Love and Mutual Benefit

Crucially, Mozi derives Heaven's moral will through observation of its actions. Heaven provides for all—great and small nations alike are its territories, and all people, regardless of age or status, are its subjects. Heaven therefore "collectively possesses and collectively feeds" all beings. From this, Mozi concludes that Heaven desires mutual love (相愛) and mutual benefit (相利), and opposes mutual hatred and mutual harm.

Historical evidence supports this: the sage-kings Yu, Tang, Wen, and Wu practiced universal love for the people, revered Heaven, and honored spirits—and were blessed with prosperity. Conversely, the tyrants Jie, Zhou, You, and Li hated the people, reviled Heaven, and insulted the spirits—and were cursed with destruction.

This chapter forms the foundation for Mozi's concepts of [[jian-ai]] (Universal Love) and [[mozi-heaven-will-tianzhi]] (Heaven's Will), establishing Heaven as the cosmic guarantor of moral order.

### 標準之必要性

墨子開篇即斷言：「天下從事者，不可以無法儀。」沒有標準，任何事業都不能成功。即使是將相這樣的人物也需要遵循法度，而各行各業的工匠同樣各有其法：百工以矩畫方，以規畫圓，以繩取直，以水測平，以縣（懸錘）正物。無論巧匠還是拙工，皆以此五者爲準則。巧者能夠命中目標，拙者雖不能中，依循從事，仍强於放任自流。

### 對人事標準之批判

墨子進而系統地駁斥了以父母、學者、君主作爲標準的可能性。他指出：天下父母雖衆，仁者寡；若皆法其父母，則是效法不仁之法，不仁之法不可爲標準。學者與君主亦然——天下爲學者衆而仁者寡，爲君者衆而仁者寡。故父母、學、君三者皆不可爲治法。

### 天爲最高標準

墨子提出的解決方案是「莫若法天」——以天爲法。天具有獨特的德性：天之行廣而無私，其施予厚重而不自居功德，其光明歷久不衰。此三者使天成爲理想標準的來源。聖王皆取法於天。

### 天志：兼愛交利

墨子通過天的行爲推導其意志：天覆育天下，無論大國小國皆天之邑，無論長幼貴賤皆天之臣。天「兼而有之、兼而食之」，故天必欲人之相愛相利，而不欲人之相惡相賊。歷史證明此理：禹湯文武兼愛天下百姓，尊天事鬼，故天福之，使立爲天子；桀紂幽厲兼惡天下百姓，詬天侮鬼，故天禍之，使其失國亡身。此篇章構成墨子「兼愛」與「天志」思想的重要基礎。

## English

Mozi's "Standard of Law" (法儀) is a foundational chapter in the Mozi text that establishes Heaven (天) as the ultimate standard for human law and governance. The chapter opens with the principle that "nothing can be accomplished without standards" (無法儀而其事能成者，無有), arguing that all crafts and professions—from craftsmen using ruler, compass, and level to generals and ministers—require established norms to succeed.

Mozi systematically eliminates three potential sources of moral and legal standards: parents, teachers, and rulers. While these figures are numerous, most are not benevolent (仁者寡), making them unsuitable as ultimate standards. An unvirtuous standard produces unvirtuous results; therefore, parents, teachers, and rulers cannot serve as the foundation for law.

The solution lies in Heaven as the supreme standard. Heaven possesses three essential qualities: broad impartiality (行廣而無私), generous施恩 without seeking recognition (施厚而不德), and enduring clarity (其明久而不衰). These divine attributes make Heaven the perfect model for human law.

The chapter establishes a crucial link between Heaven's nature and human ethics. Since Heaven universally sustains all nations and peoples without exception, and since Heaven desires mutual love and mutual benefit (相愛相利) while rejecting mutual harm and mutual贼害 (相惡相賊), the law must reflect these heavenly preferences. [[mozis-heaven-will-tianzhi]] develops this connection between Heaven's will and moral governance.

Historical examples validate this framework. The sage kings Yu, Tang, Wen, and Wu practiced [[jian-ai|universal love]] (兼愛) toward all people, honored Heaven and spirits, and were rewarded with prosperity and rule. Conversely, Jie, Zhou, You, and Li hated all people, insulted Heaven and spirits, and were ultimately destroyed. This pattern demonstrates that following Heaven's standard brings blessing, while departing from it brings calamity.

The chapter establishes that law must align with Heaven's impartial love rather than human authority or familial loyalty.

## 中文

《墨子·法儀》為《墨子》一書中的重要篇章，系統闡述了以天為最高法儀的思想。全篇以「天下從事者，不可以無法儀」開篇，確立了無法則事不成的基本前提。墨子認為，不僅工匠需用矩尺、圓規、繩墨、水平、懸垂以正其術將相亦需有法，乃至百工之事皆有法度可循。

本章最具哲學深度的論證在於层层递进的排除法。墨子首先考察父母、學者、君主三者是否可作為治法之標準。他指出，天下為父母者眾而仁者寡，若皆法其父母，則此法不仁；天下為學者眾而仁者寡，若皆法其學，則此法不仁；天下為君者眾而仁者寡，若皆法其君，則此法亦不仁。「法不仁，不可以為法」這一論證，體現了墨子以仁與否作為標準之標準的元倫理學立場。

由此，墨子提出「莫若法天」的核心命題。天之行具有三重特質：行廣而無私，體現普遍性與公正性；施厚而不德，顯示厚施而不求回報之崇高；其明久而不衰，表明永恆不變之真理。聖王正是法天而立治。

天的本質規定性表現在其意欲之上：天必欲人之相愛相利，而不欲人之相惡相賊。墨子以「兼而愛之、兼而利之」、「兼而有之、兼而食之」說明天對天下人一視同仁之養育，以證成天之兼愛本性。此與[[mozis-heaven-will-tianzhi|天志]]思想一脈相承，共同構成墨子哲學的宗教倫理基礎。

歷史驗證亦強化此論述。禹湯文武兼愛天下百姓，尊天事鬼，故天福之而立為天子；桀紂幽厲兼惡天下百姓，詬天侮鬼，故天禍之而失國亡身。此一善有善報、惡有惡報之歷史模式，印證了「愛人利人者，天必福之；惡人賊人者，天必禍之」的天道因果觀。

《法儀》篇確立了墨子政治哲學的基本框架：以天志為最高法源，以兼愛為核心價值，以歷史因果為驗證標準，為其「上说下教」的政治實踐提供哲學依據。

## 日本語

『墨子·法儀』は中国古代哲学の重要篇章であり、天を法と治世の最高基準として確立する。篇の冒頭において墨子は「天下从事者不可以無法儀」と宣言し、法の標準なくして事成り能わざることを根本原理として提示する。工匠が矩尺・円規・水準器・錘糸を用いて職を成すように、将相も亦法を有し、庶民工商も万事法に則って事を行う。

本章の哲学的中核は排除法による論証にある。墨子は父母・学者・君主の三者を順に検討し、それぞれが治法の基準となり得ないことを論じる。天下の父母は多数あれど仁者は少なし。父母を法とすれば不仁之法となる。同様に、学者を法とすれば不仁之法となり、君主を法とすれば同样に不仁之法となる。「法不仁不可以為法」——仁ならざる法は法たり得ない。この論証は、仁与否を以って法之法性を判定するというメタ倫理的立場を示す。

かくして墨子は「莫若法天」という核心命題に至る。天の行いは三つの特質を持つ。行廣而無私は普遍性と公正性を表し、施厚而不徳は厚く施すも徳と求めぬ高潔さを示し、其明久而不衰は永遠不変の真理性を意味한다。聖王はまさに天を法として治を立つ。

天の本质は其意欲に表れる。天は必ず人の相爱相助を欲し、人の相悪相賊を欲せぬ。天下の大小国家は皆天之城邑であり、人間の長幼貴賤は皆天之臣民である。天は兼而有之兼而食之——万民を一視同仁に養育する。故に、天は天下人が相爱相助するを欲し、相悪相賊するのを用ぬ。这是墨子の[[mozis-heaven-will-tianzhi|天志]]思想の根本依据である。

歴史的事例はこの枠組みを検証する。禹・湯・文・武の聖王は天下の百姓を兼愛し、天と鬼を尊び事えたので、天は之を福し天子たらしめた。桀・紂・幽・厲の暴王は天下の百姓を兼悪し、天と鬼を侮ったので、天は之を禍し国家を失った。「善人利人者は天必ず之を福し、悪人賊人者は天必ず之を禍す」という因果応報の法則は、法が天の意志に適合すべきことを歴史的に証明する。

法儀篇は墨子政治哲学の基本枠組みを確立した。以て天志を最高法源とし、以て兼愛を核心価値とし、以て歴史因果を検証基準とする。
