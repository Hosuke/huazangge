## English

The practice of self-examination ("三省吾身") represents a foundational methodological principle in Confucian moral cultivation, articulated by曾子 (Zengzi) in the Analects: "吾日三省乎吾身：为人谋而不忠乎？与朋友交而不信乎？传不习乎？" (I daily examine myself on three points: whether in transacting business for others I may have been faithful; whether in intercourse with friends I may have been sincere; whether I may have not reviewed and practiced what the teacher taught).

This practice embodies the Confucian emphasis on introspective self-cultivation as opposed to external regulation. The three areas of examination—loyalty in serving others, sincerity in friendships, and diligent review of teachings—cover the essential domains of social interaction: professional duty, personal relationships, and intellectual development.

The significance of this practice extends beyond mere self-criticism. It represents an active engagement with one's own moral development, a continuous process of evaluating whether one's actions align with Confucian ethical standards. This introspective approach became a model for subsequent Confucian generations, emphasizing the importance of personal responsibility in moral growth.

Furthermore, this practice connects to the broader Confucian concept of "修己" (self-cultivation), which forms the foundation for "治人" (governing others). The Analects frequently emphasizes that one's ability to properly govern depends fundamentally on the quality of one's own moral cultivation. Without rigorous self-examination, one cannot hope to effectively guide or lead others.

The practice also reflects the Confucian belief in the perfectibility of human nature through sustained effort. Unlike fatalistic views that consider moral development predetermined, Confucian self-examination assumes that individuals can continuously improve through deliberate reflection and correction of their shortcomings.

## 中文

"三省吾身"作为儒家修养方法的基本原则，载于《论语·学而》，由曾子提出："吾日三省乎吾身：为人谋而不忠乎？与朋友交而不信乎？传不习乎？"

这一修身方法体现了儒家内省修身的核心理念，强调道德修养源于内心的自觉反思，而非外在的强制约束省察的三个方面——为人谋是否忠诚、与朋友交是否诚信、传习是否复习——涵盖了社会交往的核心领域：职业责任、人际关系与学问进修。

此修习方法的意义远超一般性的自我批评。它代表了对自身道德发展的积极投入，是一个持续评估自身行为是否符合儒家伦理标准的过程。这种内省方法成为后世儒家的典范，强调个人在道德成长中的主观能动性。

此外，这一修习方法与儒家"修己治人"的整体理念相联系。《论语》频繁强调，个人治理他人的能力根本取决于自身道德修养的质量。没有严格的自我省察，便无法有效指导或领导他人。

此修习方法亦反映了儒家关于人性可通过持续努力趋于完善的思想。与认为道德发展早已注定的宿命论观点不同，儒家自我省察假定个人可通过有意识的反思与改过而不断进步。

## 日本語

「三省吾身」（毎日三度自己を省みる）是正統な儒教的道徳修養の基本原則であり、『論語·学而』に曾子によって表述されている。「吾日三省乎吾身。為人謀而不忠乎。与朋友交而不信乎。伝不習乎」（私は毎日三つの点を自省する。他人のために謀りを务めるにおいて忠実であったか。朋友との交わりにおいて真诚であったか。先生が教えたことを復習し実行したか）。

この修養の方法は、儒教の内省的自己修養という中心的な理念を体現しており、道徳修養が外部の強制ではなく、内面的な自覚的反思から生じることを強調している。省察の三つの領域——他人への奉仕における忠実さ、友情における真诚、教授されたことの復習——は、社会交流の本質的な領域を網羅している：職業的責任、人間関係、知的発展。

この実践の意味は単なる自己批判を超える。それは儒教的倫理基準に合わせて行動是否を評価する積極的な自己の道徳的発展への関与であり、継続的なプロセスである。この内省的な方法は後の儒教世代の手本となり、道徳的成長における個人的責任の重要性を強調した。

さらに、この方法は儒教の「修己」（自己修養）というより広範な概念联系起来おり、「治人」（他人を治める）の基盤を形成する。『論語』はしばしば、他人を適切に統治する能力は基本的に自己の道徳的修養の質に依存することを強調している。厳格な自己省察がなければ、他人を効果的に指導したり領導したりすることは期望できない。

この実践は、持続的な努力によって人性の完全性が達成可能であるという儒教の信念も反映している。道徳的発展が前定了されているとする宿命論的見解とは対照的に、儒教の自己省察は、個人意識的な反思と欠点の修正を通じて継続的に改善できることを前提としている。

## English

# Confucian Self-Examination

**Confucian self-examination** refers to the disciplined practice of daily moral introspection central to Confucian ethical cultivation. This practice enables individuals to monitor their moral progress and correct shortcomings.

## Zengzi's Threefold Examination

The most famous formulation comes from Zengzi: "吾日三省吾身：為人謀而不忠乎？與朋友交而不信乎？傳不習乎?" (I daily examine myself on three points: In planning for others, have I been disloyal? In intercourse with friends, have I been untrustworthy? And have I failed to practice what has been transmitted?)

This examination covers three key relationships:
- **Loyalty in service** to superiors
- **Trustworthiness** in friendships  
- **Practice** of transmitted teachings

## Connection to Moral Development

Self-examination serves as the foundation for becoming a [[junzi]]. Through constant reflection, individuals develop [[moral-responsibility]] and cultivate virtues like [[benevolence]]. The practice aligns with the Confucian emphasis on practical ethics over abstract speculation.

## Practical Implementation

The examination is not merely intellectual but involves sincere commitment to improvement. It represents the internalization of Confucian values and the active pursuit of moral perfection through daily habit formation.

## English

### The Practice of Self-Reflection

Confucian self-examination involves constant introspection and moral vigilance. As Confucius advises: "The Master said, 'When we see men of worth, we should think of equaling them; when we see men of a contrary character, we should turn inwards and examine ourselves'" (Analects 4.17).

### Methods of Self-Cultivation

Key practices include:
- Learning from others: Using both positive and negative examples for self-improvement
- Moral vigilance: "The Master said, 'In the morning, hear the Way; in the evening, die content!'" (Analects 4.8)
- Speech restraint: "The Master said, 'The superior man wishes to be slow in his speech and earnest in his conduct'" (Analects 4.24)

### The Way of Constant Improvement

This practice connects to the broader [[confucian-ethics]] framework, emphasizing continuous moral development rather than fixed achievement. The focus remains on personal cultivation rather than external recognition: "The Master said, 'The scholar who is intent on learning the truth, yet is ashamed of poor clothes and poor food, is not fit to be discoursed with'" (Analects 4.9).

## English

# Self-Examination

Self-examination (自省) represents a fundamental Confucian practice of introspection and moral cultivation through daily reflection on one's thoughts, words, and actions.

## Conceptual Foundation

The practice originates from Confucius's teachings in the Analects, where he emphasizes the importance of regular self-assessment as a means of moral development. This aligns with broader [[confucian-ethics]] that prioritize personal virtue cultivation.

## Methodological Approach

Confucian self-examination involves three key dimensions:
- Reflection on daily conduct and interactions
- Assessment against moral standards of [[benevolence]] and righteousness
- Identification of areas requiring improvement

## Philosophical Significance

This practice serves as the foundation for [[confucian-ethics]] in the Confucian tradition, enabling individuals to progress toward the ideal of [[junzi]] (the noble person). It represents an internal mechanism for moral accountability that complements external rituals and social norms.

# Confucian Self-Examination

Confucian self-examination constitutes a fundamental practice in the Confucian path of moral cultivation, where individuals regularly reflect on their actions, intentions, and character to align with virtuous conduct.

## Daily Introspection

The modern verification challenge prompting users to "confirm that you are a human user" metaphorically echoes the Confucian practice of constant self-examination. As Zengzi, Confucius's disciple, stated: "I daily examine myself on three points..." (*Analects* 1.4), emphasizing regular moral accounting.

## Authenticity Verification

The requirement to prove one's humanity through specific actions parallels Confucian concerns about authentic versus artificial virtue. The text's distinction between "human user" and "automated program" reflects Confucian distinction between genuine [[benevolence]] and mere performance of ritual.

## Trust Through Demonstrated Character

The text's explanation that "trust is calculated based on a number of indicators, primarily the prior actions of users" directly corresponds to Confucian understanding that moral credibility develops through consistent demonstration of virtuous behavior over time.
