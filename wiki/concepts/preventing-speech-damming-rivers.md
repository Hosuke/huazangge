## English

"Preventing People's Speech is Harder than Damming Rivers" (防民之口甚於防川) is one of the most celebrated political metaphors in Chinese classical literature, originating from the Guoyu (Discourses of the States) and later cited in the Zuo Zhuan. The statement was made by Duke Shao (邵公, also known as Duke Mu of Shao or Shao Hu) when advising King Li of Zhou about the dangers of suppressing public criticism.

### Historical Context

During King Li's reign (857-842 BCE), the king grew increasingly tyrannical and the common people began openly criticizing his rule. When informed that the people could no longer endure his oppressive orders, King Li became furious. He employed a sorcerer from Wei to monitor and report those who spoke against him, leading to executions. The people dared not speak freely, communicating only through meaningful glances in the streets.

When Duke Shao learned of this, he warned: "You think you have silenced the criticisms, but in fact you have merely blocked them." He then delivered his famous argument comparing the dangers of suppressing public opinion to the catastrophic consequences of damming a river.

### The River Metaphor

Duke Shao explained: "To control the people by silencing them is more dangerous than damming a river. When a river is dammed, the water backs up and eventually breaks through, causing immense damage to people below. The same principle applies to controlling speech."

His solution was not to prevent people from speaking, but to guide their expression through proper channels: "Therefore, those who govern waters must direct them; those who govern the people must guide them to speak." He then outlined the complete system of political feedback: officials at various levels—from ministers to common people—would offer criticism through poetry, counsel, historical records, and direct petitions. Only through this system could governance be harmonious and avoid mistakes.

### Legacy and Influence

This passage has been enormously influential in Chinese political thought. The phrase "防民之口甚於防川" became a standard reference for warning against the suppression of criticism. Later Confucian scholars frequently cited it to admonish rulers. The Han Dynasty historian Sima Qian included an expanded version in his Records of the Grand Historian, and the passage appears in the influential anthology Wenti Leijian.

Duke Shao's framework also established a foundational model for legitimate political consultation, with its multi-tiered system of feedback through different social classes and official positions. This represents one of the earliest articulations of what would later be developed into the Confucian ideal of [[governance-virtue-de|governance through virtue]] rather than coercion.

## 中文

「防民之口甚於防川」是中國古代政治哲學中最著名的警句之一，出自《國語·周語上》，為邵公（邵穆公）勸諫厲王勿止謗時所言。此語以江河氾濫為喻，闡明遏絕民言之危險，成為後世諫諍箴規之典範。

### 歷史背景

周厲王暴虐，國人憤怨，皆以言論譏刺時政。厲王聞之震怒，乃得衛巫使其監謗，國人遂不敢言，道路以目。邵公見此情形，乃進諫曰：「民不堪命矣！」厲王非但不納，反以為已止謗成功，洋洋得意。邵公遂以此著名比喻警醒厲王。

### 江河之喻

邵公之言曰：「防民之口，甚於防川。川壅而潰，傷人必多，民亦如之。」此以水道為喻：堵塞河流必致決堤之災，堵塞言路亦必致民怨沸騰。其救治之道，在「決之使導」，而非「壅之使塞」；在「宣之使言」，而非禁之使緘。

邵公並具體闡述聽政之制：自公卿至列士皆可獻詩諷諫，瞽者獻樂，史官進書，師箴之，瞍賦之，矇誦之，百工諫議，庶人傳語，近臣盡規，親戚補察，最後由王斟酌採納。如此則「事行而不悖」，政通人和。

### 思想意涵

此說奠定了儒家以德治國、民本思想之基礎。邵公之論可視為原始儒學中「天視自我民視，天聽自我民聽」觀念之具體闡發。其強調君王當虚心納諫，廣開言路，不可恃威權以壓制民意。此種政治智慧對後世影響深遠，成為歷代諫臣規勸君王之標準論述。

## 日本語

「防民之口甚於防川」（国民の口を塞ぐことは、川をせき止めるよりも難しい）は、中国古典文献における最も著名な政治的メタファーの一つであり、『国語』周語上に由来する。邵公（召穆公）が周の厲王に人民の批判を弾圧することの危険性を警告した際に発せられた言葉である。

### 歴史的背景

厲王（在位：紀元前857年 - 紀元前842年）の治世において、王は次第に暴政を行い、人民は公然と批判するようになった。邵公は人民の口を塞ぐことが川をせき止めることよりも危険であると警告し、著名な議論を展開した。

### 川の比喩

邵公は，「川を堰き止めれば水は溢れ出し、多くの人に被害をもたらす。人民の口を塞ぐことも同じ道理である」と論じた。その解決策は発言を阻止することではなく、「良い言葉は広く聞かせ、悪い言葉は修正する」という適切な制度を通じて人民の声を導くことであった。

### 遺産と影響

この言葉は中国，政治思想において非常に影響力を持つようになり、批判の弾圧に対する警告の標準的な参照となった。邵公の枠組みはまた、適切な政治的意思疎通のモデルとして機能し、後の儒教の徳治主義の理想の基礎を築いた。
