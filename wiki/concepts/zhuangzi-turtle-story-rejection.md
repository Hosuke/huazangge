## English

The **Turtle Story** (曳尾塗中) is one of the most famous anecdotes from the *Zhuangzi*, appearing in Chapter 17 (Autumn Floods). It illustrates the core Daoist value of preferring freedom and spontaneity over political power and social status.

### The Story

The King of Chu (楚王), impressed with Zhuangzi's reputation, sent two high officials to invite Zhuangzi to serve as minister: "The King wishes to trouble you with the administration of his realm."

Zhuangzi, however, was fishing in the Pu River (濮水) and ignored the officials. Without turning his head, he said: "I have heard that the Chu has a sacred turtle, which has been dead for three thousand years. The King keeps it wrapped in cloth and stored in a bamboo box in the ancestral temple. Is this turtle better off dead and preserved as a sacred relic, or alive and dragging its tail in the mud?"

The officials replied: "It would rather be alive and dragging its tail in the mud."

Zhuangzi said: "Go away! I will drag my tail in the mud."

### Philosophical Significance

This story crystallizes several key Daoist principles:

**Preferring Freedom over Honor:** The sacred turtle represents those who accept high positions—they become "preserved" like specimens in a museum, losing their freedom and authenticity. Zhuangzi chooses the humble but free life of a turtle in the mud.

**Critique of Political Engagement:** The story reflects Zhuangzi's consistent critique of political involvement. Unlike [[confucian-political-philosophy|Confucian]] engagement with the world, Daoists advocate [[wuyong-zhi-yong|withdrawal from political life]].

**Wu-wei (無為):** By refusing to "help" the king govern, Zhuangzi practices [[dao-de-jing-complete|non-action]]—allowing things to follow their natural course rather than interfering through artificial means.

**Spontaneity (自然):** The turtle dragging its tail in the mud represents living according to [[dao-de-jing-complete|nature]], unencumbered by social expectations or political responsibilities.

### Cultural Impact

This story has become a touchstone of Chinese cultural attitudes toward official service versus personal freedom. The phrase "drag tail in mud" (曳尾塗中) has become an idiom meaning "to prefer a humble but free life over an honorable but constrained position."

The story is often juxtaposed with the Confucian ideal of [[governance-virtue-de|governing through virtue]] and serving for the common good, representing the fundamental tension between Daoist and [[confucian-political-philosophy|Confucian]] approaches to public service.

## 中文

**曳尾塗中**是《莊子》秋水篇中最著名的寓言之一，也是理解道家政治哲學的關鍵文本。它生動地展示了道家「無用之用」的核心價值——寧為自由之魚，不為顯貴之龜。

### 故事經過

楚王派遣兩位大夫前往請莊子入朝為相，曰：「願以境內累矣！」——願將國家政務委託於莊子。

莊子正在濮水邊持竿垂釣，對兩位大夫不予理會，頭也不回地說：「吾聞楚有神龜，死已三千歲矣，王巾笥而藏之廟堂之上。此龜者，寧其死為留骨而貴乎？寧其生而曳尾於塗中乎？」

兩位大夫回答：「寧生而曳尾塗中。」

莊子說：「往矣！吾將曳尾於塗中。」

### 哲學意涵

這個故事凝結了道家思想的幾個核心原則：

**寧為自由賤役，不為顯貴之囚：** 神龜象徵那些接受高位的人——他們雖被奉於廟堂，卻失去了行動自由與本性真實。莊子選擇做一隻在泥濘中曳尾的自由烏龜。

**對政治參與的批判：** 這個故事反映了莊子對政治 involvement一貫的批判態度。與[[confucian-political-philosophy|儒家]]積極入世不同，道家主張遠離政治紛爭。

**無為（無為）：** 拒絕「幫助」君王治理，莊子實踐了[[dao-de-jing-complete|無為]]——讓萬物自然發展，而非透過人為手段干預。

**自然（自然）：** 曳尾於塗中的烏龜代表順[[dao-de-jing-complete|自然]]而活，不受社會期望或政治責任的束縛。

### 文化影響

這個故事已成為中國文化中對待仕途與自由態度的試金石。「曳尾塗中」成為慣用語，意指「寧為賤役而自由，不為顯貴而受束縛」。

此故事常與儒家「[[governance-virture-de|為政以德]]」及「兼濟天下」的理想形成對比，代表了道家與[[confucian-political-philosophy|儒家]]處理公共服務的根本分歧。
