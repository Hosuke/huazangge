## English  

### Definition  
**Benevolent Governance** (仁政, *rénzhèng*) is a central concept in Confucian political thought that describes a form of rule in which the ruler governs with benevolence, moral virtue, and a sincere concern for the welfare of the people. It stands in contrast to coercive or purely legalistic approaches to government, emphasizing that political authority must be grounded in the ruler’s moral self‑cultivation and the practice of *ren* (仁, humaneness).  

### Historical Origins  
- **Confucius (551‑479 BC)**: Though the term “仁政” does not appear verbatim in the *Analects*, Confucius laid the groundwork by teaching that a ruler must “govern by moral force” (德治) and that “the government must be like a father to the people.”  
- **Mencius (372‑289 BC)**: The classic articulation of *renzhèng* is found in Mencius, who argued that a king’s legitimacy derives from the material and moral well‑being of his subjects. In *Mencius* 1A‑3, he famously states: “The people are the root of a state; when the root is secure, the state is at peace.”  
- **Later Developments**: The Han dynasty’s **Dong Zhongshu** and the Song Neo‑Confucians (e.g., Zhu Xi) reinterpreted *renzhèng* within a cosmic‑moral framework, linking it to the “Mandate of Heaven” (天命) and the principle that a ruler’s virtue can harmonize the universe.  

### Core Principles  
1. **People‑Oriented (以民为本)**: The welfare of the populace is the primary purpose of government.  
2. **Moral Self‑Cultivation (修身齐家)**: The ruler must first cultivate personal virtue before expecting societal order.  
3. **Humane Policies (仁爱惠民)**: Measures such as low taxes, public works, and grain relief embody compassion.  
4. **Education and Ritual (教化礼仪)**: The government should promote moral education and proper ritual to foster social harmony.  
5. **Responsive Governance (因时制宜)**: Policies must adapt to the needs of the people, reflecting practical wisdom rather than rigid legalism.  

### Key Passages from the Classical Canon  
- **Mencius 1A‑7**: “If the ruler loves the people, the people will love him; if he does not, the people will hate him.”  
- **Mencius 3A‑4**: “When the granaries are full, the people know propriety; when the coffers are empty, they know modesty.”  
- **Xunzi (《荀子》)**: “A ruler who practices *ren* will have the support of Heaven and the loyalty of the people.”  
- **Liji (《礼记》) – “Yue Ling”**: “The king’s government is the people’s government; the people are the roots of the state.”  

### Relationship to Other Confucian Concepts  
- **仁爱 (Renai)**: The personal virtue of benevolence that underlies *renzhèng*.  
- **君子 (Junzi)**: The morally cultivated individual who, when in power, naturally enacts *renzhèng*.  
- **礼 (Li)**: The ritual and propriety that give form to benevolent policies.  
- **义 (Yi)**: The sense of righteousness that guides the ruler’s decisions in the interest of the people.  

### Implementation in Practice  
- **Agricultural Support**: Provision of land, irrigation, and “five acres and a mulberry tree” to ensure food security.  
- **Tax Relief**: “轻徭薄赋” (light conscription, low taxes) to avoid overburdening the peasantry.  
- **Public Works**: Construction of granaries, flood‑control projects, and roads to safeguard livelihoods.  
- **Moral Education**: Establishment of schools and promotion of Confucian classics to cultivate virtue among the populace.  

### Influence and Legacy  
- **Imperial Practice**: Throughout Chinese history, dynastic legitimization often invoked the language of *renzhèng* to justify reforms (e.g., the Tang’s equal‑field system, the Song’s “Qingli” reforms).
