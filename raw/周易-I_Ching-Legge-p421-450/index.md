According to paragraph 63 of last Appendix, Section ii, both 
the third and fourth lines in the hexagram belong to man, and are 
sntermediate between those of heaven and those of earth. 
Khung 
Ying-t4, to get over the difficulty in what is said on the fourth

it. 
The subject of this fourth line will move very 
cautiously, and so escape blame. 
The eulogium of ‘the great man’ in paragraph 34 cannot fail to 
recall to the classical scholar the thirty-first and other chapters of ‘ the 
Doctrine of the Mean,’ where the sage is described as ‘The Equal 
of Heaven.’ 
In one sentence here he is spoken of as sometimes 
taking precedence of Heaven, which then does not act in opposi- 
tion to him! 
I do not know of any statement about the sage, 
coming without doubt from Confucius, that is so extravagant as 
this. It is difficult—in fact impossible—to say from the Y1 itself, what 
we are to understand by the kwei shin, which I have translated 
here by ‘the spirit-like operations (of Providence). 
The compound 
denomination does not often occur in the book.

Section i, 21, kwei is the anima and shin the animus; and 
in paragraph 50, I have translated the terms by ‘ the contracting and 
expanding operations.’
